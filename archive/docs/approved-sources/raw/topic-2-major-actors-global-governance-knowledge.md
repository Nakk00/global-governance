# Topic 2: Major Actors in Global Governance

## Short Overview

Global governance is not carried out by one world authority. It is shaped by many actors that cooperate, compete, negotiate, regulate, monitor, finance, interpret, implement, and sometimes obstruct collective responses to global problems.

An actor in global governance is any state, institution, organization, group, network, company, community, or individual that can influence how cross-border issues are understood, managed, or resolved. Some actors have formal legal authority. Others influence global governance through expertise, money, advocacy, public pressure, technology, military power, moral legitimacy, or control over information.

The major actors include sovereign states, international organizations, regional organizations, courts and tribunals, non-governmental organizations, civil society groups, multinational corporations, expert networks, philanthropic foundations, social movements, local governments, leaders, citizens, and even harmful actors such as armed groups or criminal networks. These actors do not all have the same power. They also do not all have the same responsibilities. Some make rules, some implement rules, some monitor compliance, some provide resources, some create knowledge, and others challenge existing systems.

The main idea is that global governance is multi-actor and networked. States remain central because they control territory, make treaties, fund institutions, and provide legal authority. However, states cannot manage global problems alone. International organizations need state support, NGOs need access and protection, experts need political uptake, corporations need regulation, and local governments often implement global commitments on the ground.

Understanding the major actors in global governance helps explain why international cooperation is useful but difficult. Global problems often require many actors to work together, but those actors may have different interests, unequal power, conflicting values, limited resources, and different ideas about what should be done.

## Core Explanation: What Is an Actor in Global Governance?

An actor in global governance is a participant that can shape international rules, decisions, policies, norms, knowledge, or behavior. Actors may be formal or informal. They may be public or private. They may operate at the local, national, regional, or global level.

A government negotiating a treaty is an actor. A United Nations agency coordinating disaster relief is an actor. A court interpreting international law is an actor. A human rights organization reporting abuses is an actor. A company setting supply-chain standards is an actor. A group of scientists producing climate assessments is an actor. A city government implementing climate policy is also an actor.

Actors can influence global governance through different kinds of power:

- **Legal authority:** the ability to create, interpret, or apply rules.
- **Political authority:** the ability to represent states, communities, or institutions.
- **Economic power:** control over money, trade, investment, loans, or markets.
- **Military power:** the ability to use or threaten force.
- **Expertise:** specialized knowledge that guides policy.
- **Moral authority:** credibility based on humanitarian, ethical, or rights-based claims.
- **Agenda-setting power:** the ability to decide which issues receive attention.
- **Implementation capacity:** the ability to turn agreements into action.
- **Public legitimacy:** acceptance or support from people, communities, or member states.

### State Actors, Intergovernmental Actors, and Non-State Actors

A **state actor** is a sovereign state or government institution. States include national governments, ministries, diplomats, militaries, courts, legislatures, and regulatory agencies. States are central because they possess sovereignty and usually have the legal authority to sign treaties, join international organizations, enforce domestic law, control borders, and collect taxes.

An **intergovernmental actor** is an organization created by states. Examples include the United Nations, World Trade Organization, World Health Organization, International Monetary Fund, World Bank, ASEAN, African Union, European Union, and Organization of American States. These institutions exist because states created them to solve shared problems or coordinate action.

A **non-state actor** is an actor that is not a sovereign government. This includes NGOs, civil society organizations, multinational corporations, social movements, philanthropic foundations, expert networks, media organizations, cities, and sometimes armed groups or criminal networks. Non-state actors do not usually make international law in the same way states do, but they can influence agendas, provide knowledge, mobilize public pressure, deliver services, shape markets, and monitor behavior.

### Why Global Governance Is Multi-Actor and Networked

Modern global governance is multi-actor because modern global problems are complex. A pandemic is not only a medical issue. It involves public health agencies, national governments, local governments, scientists, pharmaceutical companies, logistics providers, humanitarian groups, schools, employers, media, and citizens. Climate change is not only an environmental issue. It involves states, cities, companies, energy systems, scientists, courts, indigenous communities, farmers, activists, investors, and international organizations.

Global governance is also networked because actors often work through relationships rather than a single chain of command. A United Nations conference may bring together states, agencies, NGOs, companies, experts, and local governments. A climate policy may require international agreements, national laws, city implementation, scientific data, corporate reporting, public pressure, and financing. A humanitarian response may require cooperation among the UN, host governments, donors, NGOs, local communities, and security actors.

This networked structure makes global governance flexible, but it also makes it complicated. When many actors are involved, coordination can be slow. Responsibilities may overlap. Some actors may have more influence than others. Some may lack accountability. Some may have good intentions but limited capacity. Others may block cooperation because they benefit from the current system or disagree with proposed rules.

## Sovereign States and National Governments

Sovereign states are the most important traditional actors in global governance. A state has authority over a defined territory and population, and it is recognized as legally independent in the international system. States remain central because international law, diplomacy, treaties, and most international organizations are built around state consent and state participation.

States are the actors that usually sign treaties, vote in international organizations, create foreign policy, deploy military forces, regulate borders, collect taxes, fund international institutions, enforce domestic laws, and represent national interests in negotiations. Even when non-state actors are influential, they often depend on states to create legal frameworks, provide funding, grant access, or enforce decisions.

### Why States Remain Central

States remain central for several reasons.

First, states have **sovereignty**. Sovereignty means that a state has authority over its own territory and political system. This gives states a special legal status that most other actors do not have.

Second, states have **treaty-making power**. International law often depends on states agreeing to legal obligations. Climate agreements, trade agreements, security alliances, human rights treaties, maritime law, and arms-control agreements are usually negotiated and accepted by states.

Third, states control many of the resources needed for global governance. They can provide funding, personnel, military support, humanitarian aid, technical expertise, and domestic implementation. International organizations often depend on member-state contributions and cooperation.

Fourth, states are responsible for domestic implementation. A global agreement only matters if it is translated into national laws, regulations, budgets, institutions, and enforcement systems. For example, climate targets require national energy policies. Human rights treaties require domestic protections. Trade agreements require customs rules and regulatory changes.

Fifth, states shape legitimacy. International institutions gain much of their authority because states recognize them. When states cooperate, global governance becomes stronger. When states refuse to cooperate, global governance often becomes weaker.

### Tools Used by States

States influence global governance through many tools:

- **Diplomacy:** negotiations, dialogue, mediation, summits, embassies, and diplomatic missions.
- **Treaties:** formal legal agreements between states.
- **Voting:** participation in international organizations and decision-making bodies.
- **Funding:** financial contributions to international organizations, development programs, humanitarian agencies, and peacekeeping.
- **Military power:** defense, deterrence, peacekeeping, security alliances, or coercive force.
- **Economic policy:** trade rules, sanctions, tariffs, development aid, loans, and investment.
- **Domestic law:** national legislation that implements international obligations.
- **Recognition:** acceptance or rejection of states, governments, boundaries, courts, or legal claims.
- **Coalition-building:** working with allies, regional partners, or issue-based groups.

### Powerful States and Smaller States

Not all states have equal influence. Powerful states may shape global governance through military strength, economic weight, technology, diplomatic reach, permanent seats in key bodies, or control over major resources. They may influence the agenda of international institutions, shape negotiations, or block decisions that do not match their interests.

Smaller states may have less material power, but they are not powerless. They can build coalitions, use international law, work through regional organizations, lead on moral or technical issues, and use multilateral forums to amplify their voice. Small island states, for example, have played important roles in climate diplomacy because they face direct risks from sea-level rise and extreme weather. Middle and smaller powers can also influence negotiations by acting as mediators, conveners, or norm entrepreneurs.

### Examples of State Roles

In **peace and security**, states negotiate ceasefires, participate in peacekeeping, impose sanctions, join alliances, and use diplomacy to prevent conflict.

In **trade**, states negotiate market access, tariffs, subsidies, standards, and dispute settlement rules.

In **climate governance**, states submit national climate commitments, regulate emissions, finance adaptation, and negotiate international climate agreements.

In **human rights**, states ratify treaties, submit reports, create national laws, and cooperate with monitoring bodies.

In **territorial disputes**, states use diplomacy, legal claims, maritime law, negotiations, arbitration, or military presence.

### Limitations of States

States can also weaken global governance. National interest may conflict with global interest. Some states may sign agreements but fail to implement them. Powerful states may resist rules that limit their freedom. Weak states may lack capacity to comply with obligations. Rivalries among major powers can block collective action. Nationalism and domestic politics can make international cooperation unpopular.

State-based global governance is therefore both necessary and limited. States are essential because they provide authority and resources, but they can also create deadlock, inequality, and selective enforcement.

## The United Nations System

The United Nations is the most visible global organization for multilateral cooperation. It was created after World War II to help maintain international peace and security, develop friendly relations among nations, promote cooperation on international problems, and encourage respect for human rights.

The UN system is broad. It includes the main organs of the United Nations, specialized agencies, funds, programs, commissions, and related organizations. It works across issues such as peace and security, development, humanitarian action, human rights, international law, public health, food, education, labor, migration, refugees, children, environment, and culture.

The UN does not function as a world government. It cannot simply command all states. It is an organization of member states and depends heavily on their consent, funding, cooperation, and political will. However, it is still central because it provides a global forum where states can meet, debate, set agendas, create norms, authorize action, and coordinate responses.

### The General Assembly

The General Assembly is the main deliberative body of the United Nations. All UN member states are represented there. Each state has one vote, which gives the General Assembly broad legitimacy as a global forum.

The General Assembly discusses major international issues, adopts resolutions, approves the UN budget, elects members to other bodies, and provides a space where all states can express their views. Its resolutions are often not legally binding in the same way Security Council decisions can be, but they can still shape international norms, political expectations, and global agendas.

The General Assembly is important because it gives smaller and larger states a common forum. It can highlight issues that might otherwise be ignored. It can also create political pressure and signal broad international opinion.

### The Security Council

The Security Council has primary responsibility for the maintenance of international peace and security. It can authorize peacekeeping missions, impose sanctions, mandate investigations, and in some cases authorize the use of force. It has fifteen members: five permanent members and ten elected members.

The five permanent members are China, France, Russia, the United Kingdom, and the United States. Each permanent member has veto power. This means that a negative vote by one permanent member can block many substantive decisions.

The Security Council can be powerful when its members agree. However, it can be limited when major powers disagree. The veto system can prevent action even during serious crises. This is one of the clearest examples of how global governance can be shaped by power politics.

### The Economic and Social Council

The Economic and Social Council, or ECOSOC, coordinates economic, social, and related work within the UN system. It serves as a key platform for development, sustainable development, social issues, and cooperation with civil society.

ECOSOC is important because many global governance problems are not military problems. Poverty, health, education, inequality, labor, development, environment, gender equality, and technology require economic and social coordination. ECOSOC also provides channels through which non-governmental organizations can participate in UN processes through consultative status.

### The International Court of Justice

The International Court of Justice is the principal judicial organ of the United Nations. It settles legal disputes submitted by states and gives advisory opinions on legal questions referred to it by authorized UN organs and specialized agencies.

The ICJ contributes to global governance by interpreting international law and providing peaceful methods for resolving disputes. It is especially relevant when states disagree over treaties, borders, sovereignty, use of force, maritime rights, or legal responsibilities.

However, the ICJ depends on jurisdiction and state cooperation. It does not have a global police force to enforce all decisions automatically. This means the Court is important for law and legitimacy, but it is still affected by politics and compliance problems.

### The Secretariat

The Secretariat carries out the daily work of the United Nations. It is led by the Secretary-General, who acts as the UN’s chief administrative officer and often serves as a diplomatic voice in world affairs.

The Secretariat supports peace operations, prepares reports, organizes conferences, provides technical support, coordinates agencies, and helps implement UN mandates. The Secretary-General can also use public diplomacy and quiet diplomacy to draw attention to crises or encourage negotiations.

The Secretariat’s influence depends on expertise, credibility, administrative capacity, and the willingness of member states to support its work.

### The Trusteeship Council

The Trusteeship Council was created to supervise trust territories and help guide them toward self-government or independence. Its active work has largely ended because the trust territories completed that process. The Council remains one of the main organs of the UN but is no longer central in daily global governance.

### How the UN Provides Global Governance

The United Nations contributes to global governance by:

- providing a forum for diplomacy
- giving legitimacy to international action
- coordinating humanitarian and development work
- supporting peacekeeping and conflict prevention
- promoting human rights and international law
- setting global agendas and norms
- gathering data and producing reports
- helping states cooperate on shared problems

### Limitations of the UN

The UN has major limitations. It depends on member states for funding, troops, political backing, and implementation. The Security Council veto can block action. Some resolutions are not legally binding. States may ignore recommendations. UN agencies may face funding shortages or political pressure. Complex crises may exceed institutional capacity.

The UN is therefore both essential and imperfect. It is one of the most important platforms for global cooperation, but it cannot solve global problems alone.

## UN Specialized Agencies and Related Bodies

UN specialized agencies and related bodies are organizations connected to the UN system that focus on specific issue areas. They usually have technical expertise and specialized mandates. These bodies show that global governance is not only about diplomacy among states. It is also about health, labor, food, education, transportation, finance, culture, and technical standards.

Specialized agencies are usually autonomous international organizations that work with the United Nations through formal agreements. They have their own memberships, budgets, structures, and mandates, but they are part of the wider UN system.

### World Health Organization

The World Health Organization is the UN agency that connects nations, partners, and people to promote health, keep the world safe, and serve vulnerable populations. It supports disease surveillance, health standards, emergency response, research guidance, vaccination efforts, health system strengthening, and coordination during health crises.

WHO is important in global governance because diseases can cross borders quickly. Public health requires cooperation on data, early warning, laboratory standards, vaccine access, emergency declarations, and best practices. WHO can provide technical guidance, but it depends on member states for reporting, funding, implementation, and political support.

### International Labour Organization

The International Labour Organization promotes social justice and internationally recognized human and labor rights. It is known for its tripartite structure, bringing together governments, employers, and workers. This structure makes the ILO a distinctive actor because it formally includes non-governmental social partners, not only states.

The ILO contributes to global governance through labor standards, decent work agendas, social protection, workplace rights, and guidance on employment policy. Its work matters because labor conditions are linked to globalization, trade, migration, inequality, and development.

### Food and Agriculture Organization

The Food and Agriculture Organization leads international efforts to defeat hunger and improve food security. It works on agriculture, nutrition, rural development, food systems, fisheries, forestry, data, and technical assistance.

FAO is important because food insecurity is both a national and global problem. Conflict, climate change, market disruption, disease, and trade patterns can affect food supplies across borders. FAO helps states and partners respond through data, policy advice, technical support, and cooperation.

### UNESCO

UNESCO promotes cooperation in education, science, culture, communication, and information. It contributes to peace by supporting education, knowledge, cultural heritage, scientific cooperation, media development, and global norms.

UNESCO matters in global governance because knowledge, culture, and education shape long-term peace and development. Its work also shows that global governance includes soft power, identity, memory, science, and values, not only treaties or military security.

### International Maritime Organization

The International Maritime Organization focuses on shipping safety, maritime security, and prevention of pollution from ships. Maritime governance matters because international trade depends heavily on sea routes, and ships move across national jurisdictions.

The IMO contributes to global governance by setting standards for international shipping, safety, environmental protection, and maritime regulation. It is especially relevant to issues such as ocean pollution, shipping emissions, port security, and maritime law.

### International Civil Aviation Organization

The International Civil Aviation Organization supports cooperation in international civil aviation. Aviation depends on common rules for safety, navigation, airspace coordination, security, and technical standards.

ICAO shows how technical governance makes global systems possible. Air travel requires states to coordinate because aircraft cross borders constantly. Without shared standards, global aviation would be less safe and less predictable.

### World Bank Group and International Monetary Fund

The World Bank Group and IMF are related to the UN system but have distinct financial roles. The World Bank focuses on development finance, poverty reduction, infrastructure, and policy advice. The IMF focuses on monetary cooperation, financial stability, balance-of-payments support, surveillance, and economic policy advice.

They are discussed further under international financial institutions, but they also belong in this section because they show how financial governance is part of the wider institutional order.

### Why Technical Expertise Matters

Specialized agencies matter because many global problems require expert knowledge. States may agree that a problem exists, but they often need technical institutions to define standards, gather data, compare evidence, support implementation, and provide policy guidance.

Technical expertise can help make global governance more practical. However, technical bodies are not free from politics. Their budgets, priorities, and effectiveness may be shaped by member states, donors, industry pressure, or geopolitical conflict.

## International Financial Institutions

International financial institutions are major actors in global governance because they shape development, economic stability, lending, crisis response, and policy reform. They include the World Bank Group, the International Monetary Fund, and regional development banks such as the Asian Development Bank, African Development Bank, Inter-American Development Bank, and European Bank for Reconstruction and Development.

### The World Bank Group

The World Bank Group is a major development institution. It provides financing, policy advice, technical assistance, and knowledge to help countries reduce poverty and address development challenges. It supports projects in areas such as infrastructure, education, health, climate, agriculture, governance, water, energy, and social protection.

The World Bank’s influence comes from its financial resources, technical expertise, project experience, research, and relationships with governments. It can support development projects that many countries could not finance alone. It can also shape national policy through loans, conditions, advice, and development frameworks.

### The International Monetary Fund

The IMF works to support global monetary cooperation, financial stability, sustainable growth, and economic adjustment. It provides surveillance of economies, policy advice, technical assistance, and financial support to countries facing balance-of-payments problems or macroeconomic crises.

The IMF is powerful because financial crises can spread quickly across borders. When a country faces debt, currency, or banking problems, the IMF can provide emergency financing and policy guidance. Its assessments can influence markets, investors, governments, and other institutions.

### Regional Development Banks

Regional development banks support development and infrastructure within particular regions. They may understand regional conditions more closely than global institutions. They can finance roads, energy systems, water projects, education, public health, digital systems, and climate adaptation. They also help coordinate development priorities among neighboring states.

### Why Financial Institutions Are Powerful

Financial institutions are powerful because money shapes policy choices. Loans, grants, debt restructuring, investment guarantees, and technical advice can influence what governments prioritize. Development finance can help build infrastructure and reduce poverty, but it can also create dependency or conflict over policy conditions.

### Criticisms and Limitations

International financial institutions face several criticisms:

- **Conditionality:** loans may require policy changes that affect domestic priorities.
- **Unequal influence:** wealthier states may have greater voting power or informal influence.
- **Debt concerns:** borrowing can increase debt burdens if projects fail or economies weaken.
- **Representation issues:** developing countries may argue that decision-making does not fully reflect their needs.
- **Policy priorities:** critics may argue that some institutions favor market-based reforms over social protection.
- **Implementation problems:** financed projects may face corruption, environmental harm, displacement, or weak local capacity.

These criticisms do not mean financial institutions are unimportant. They mean their role must be understood carefully. They can provide essential resources and expertise, but they also raise questions about fairness, accountability, and power.

## Trade and Economic Institutions

Trade and economic institutions create rules for international commerce. They help states manage tariffs, subsidies, market access, customs, standards, intellectual property, investment, and dispute settlement. Trade governance matters because economies are interconnected. Goods, services, capital, data, labor, and supply chains cross borders.

### The World Trade Organization

The World Trade Organization is the main global body dealing with the rules of trade between nations. Its agreements provide legal ground rules for trade. The WTO gives members a forum for negotiations, trade monitoring, and dispute settlement.

The WTO contributes to global governance by making trade more predictable. Rules can reduce uncertainty, limit discrimination, and provide procedures for resolving disputes. This can support economic growth, but it also creates debates about fairness, sovereignty, development, and domestic policy space.

### Trade Rules and Dispute Settlement

Trade rules affect tariffs, import restrictions, subsidies, product standards, services, agriculture, intellectual property, and digital trade. Dispute settlement allows members to challenge trade measures they believe violate WTO agreements.

Dispute settlement is important because trade conflicts can otherwise escalate into retaliation. A rules-based system gives states a legal process instead of relying only on power. However, the effectiveness of dispute settlement depends on institutional functioning, member compliance, and political support.

### Rich and Developing Countries

Trade governance affects countries differently. Wealthier economies may have more negotiating capacity, larger markets, and stronger legal teams. Developing countries may use trade to support growth, but they may also face challenges from unequal bargaining power, limited industrial capacity, commodity dependence, or exposure to global market shocks.

Trade rules can create opportunities, but they can also restrict policy choices. Debates over agriculture, intellectual property, digital trade, industrial policy, and environmental standards often reflect different development priorities.

### Limitations of Trade Governance

Trade institutions face several limits:

- major powers may use tariffs or economic pressure outside multilateral rules
- dispute settlement can become politically contested
- negotiations can stall because members have different interests
- poorer states may lack capacity to fully participate
- domestic politics can resist trade liberalization
- trade rules may not fully address labor rights, environment, inequality, or digital power

Trade governance is therefore both a stabilizing force and a contested arena.

## Regional Organizations

Regional organizations are institutions created by states within a particular geographic region. They are important because some problems are best managed at the regional level. Neighboring countries often share security concerns, migration flows, trade links, environmental systems, cultural ties, historical disputes, and economic interests.

Regional organizations can support diplomacy, development, security, trade, human rights, disaster response, and conflict management. They can also act as bridges between national governments and global institutions.

### ASEAN

The Association of Southeast Asian Nations, or ASEAN, is a regional organization in Southeast Asia. It promotes cooperation, dialogue, regional stability, economic integration, and partnerships with other countries and organizations.

ASEAN is important for regional governance because Southeast Asia faces shared issues such as maritime disputes, trade, disaster risks, migration, transnational crime, public health, and great-power competition. ASEAN often works through dialogue, consensus, and respect for sovereignty.

ASEAN’s consensus-based approach can support unity and reduce open confrontation. However, it can also limit action when members disagree. This is especially important in sensitive issues such as human rights, security crises, and territorial disputes.

### European Union

The European Union is a regional organization with unusually deep integration. It coordinates policies among member states and has institutions that can make binding rules in areas where members have transferred authority. The EU works on trade, regulation, currency for eurozone members, human rights, environmental standards, migration, development, foreign policy coordination, and regional security.

The EU is important in global governance because it can act collectively in trade negotiations, climate policy, sanctions, development aid, data regulation, and human rights diplomacy. Its internal market gives it global regulatory influence.

However, the EU also faces limitations. Member states may disagree on migration, defense, fiscal policy, enlargement, relations with major powers, or domestic rule-of-law issues. Its influence depends on internal unity and institutional capacity.

### African Union

The African Union is a continental body consisting of African states. It works on African integration, peace and security, development, democracy, human rights, and continental cooperation.

The AU matters because many governance challenges in Africa involve cross-border security, development, health, trade, climate, migration, and political stability. It can provide regional legitimacy and African-led responses to continental problems.

The AU faces limitations such as funding challenges, member-state disagreement, capacity constraints, and difficulty enforcing decisions. Still, it remains a major regional actor because it gives African states a collective platform.

### Organization of American States

The Organization of American States brings together states of the Americas and serves as a political, legal, and social forum in the Western Hemisphere. Its pillars include democracy, human rights, security, and development.

The OAS contributes to regional governance through election observation, human rights mechanisms, democratic norms, security cooperation, and diplomatic dialogue. It can help address regional crises, but it is also affected by ideological divisions, member-state politics, and debates about sovereignty.

### Arab League

The League of Arab States, commonly known as the Arab League, is a regional organization of Arab states. It provides a forum for political coordination, cultural cooperation, and regional diplomacy.

Its influence varies depending on member agreement. It can express regional positions and support diplomatic coordination, but divisions among members often limit its effectiveness.

### NATO

The North Atlantic Treaty Organization is a political and military alliance. Its core purpose is to guarantee the freedom and security of its members through political and military means. Collective defense is central to NATO’s identity.

NATO is relevant to global governance because security governance is not limited to the UN. Alliances can shape deterrence, crisis management, military standards, defense planning, and regional security. NATO’s actions can also affect broader international politics.

However, NATO is not universal. It represents its members, not the entire international community. Its role can be controversial, especially when alliance interests conflict with other states’ security perceptions.

### Global vs Regional Governance

Global governance deals with problems at the worldwide level. Regional governance deals with problems that are especially important within a particular region. The two levels often interact. Regional organizations may implement global goals, support UN action, mediate regional disputes, or represent regional interests in global negotiations.

Regional organizations can be more familiar with local contexts, but they may also be limited by regional rivalries, sovereignty concerns, and uneven resources.

## International Courts and Tribunals

International courts and tribunals help interpret international law, settle disputes, and support accountability. They do not govern the world in the same way domestic courts govern within a country, but they contribute to global governance by providing legal processes for issues that might otherwise be handled only through power politics.

### International Court of Justice

The International Court of Justice is the principal judicial organ of the United Nations. It settles legal disputes submitted by states and gives advisory opinions on legal questions.

The ICJ is important because it allows states to use law to address disputes. Its decisions and advisory opinions can clarify legal principles, influence diplomacy, and support peaceful resolution. It can deal with questions involving borders, treaties, diplomatic relations, state responsibility, use of force, and maritime claims.

Its main limitation is jurisdiction. States generally need to consent to the Court’s jurisdiction for contentious cases. Even when the Court gives a judgment, enforcement depends heavily on state compliance and political conditions.

### International Criminal Court

The International Criminal Court investigates and, where warranted, tries individuals accused of the gravest crimes of concern to the international community. These include genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes, and the crime of aggression under defined conditions.

The ICC is different from the ICJ. The ICJ deals mainly with disputes between states. The ICC focuses on individual criminal responsibility. It is designed to complement national courts, especially when national systems are unwilling or unable to prosecute serious crimes.

The ICC contributes to global governance by supporting accountability for atrocities. However, it faces major limits. Not all states are parties to the Rome Statute. The Court depends on cooperation for arrests and evidence. It may face accusations of political bias. Powerful states may resist its authority.

### Permanent Court of Arbitration

The Permanent Court of Arbitration is an intergovernmental organization that provides dispute resolution services, including arbitration. It can support cases involving states, state entities, international organizations, and private parties.

The PCA is important because arbitration can help resolve complex disputes, including maritime boundaries, investment disputes, territorial issues, and treaty questions. It provides procedural support rather than acting like a single permanent court with compulsory jurisdiction over all states.

Like other legal mechanisms, its effectiveness depends on consent, participation, and compliance.

### Human Rights Courts and Regional Legal Bodies

Some regions have human rights courts or legal bodies. Examples include the European Court of Human Rights, Inter-American Court of Human Rights, and African human rights mechanisms. These institutions support accountability and legal interpretation within regional systems.

They can help individuals and communities challenge abuses, but their authority depends on treaties, member-state acceptance, institutional independence, and implementation.

### Limitations of International Courts

International courts face recurring limitations:

- states may not accept jurisdiction
- powerful states may resist compliance
- courts may lack direct enforcement tools
- legal processes can be slow
- decisions may become politically contested
- victims may still struggle to obtain remedies
- some actors may be outside the court’s reach

Courts are important because they make law visible and provide peaceful procedures, but they do not remove politics from global governance.

## Non-Governmental Organizations and Civil Society

Non-governmental organizations, or NGOs, are organizations that are not part of government and are usually formed around social, humanitarian, environmental, development, rights-based, professional, or advocacy goals. Civil society is broader. It includes NGOs, community groups, faith-based organizations, labor unions, professional associations, social movements, academic groups, and citizen networks.

NGOs and civil society organizations matter because global governance is not only shaped by governments. Many global issues affect people directly, and civil society groups often represent concerns that states may ignore.

### Roles of NGOs and Civil Society

NGOs and civil society organizations can play many roles:

- **Advocacy:** pushing governments and institutions to act on issues such as human rights, climate change, corruption, poverty, and conflict.
- **Humanitarian aid:** delivering food, shelter, medical care, protection, and emergency support.
- **Monitoring:** documenting abuses, corruption, environmental harm, labor conditions, or violations of international law.
- **Research:** producing reports, data, and policy analysis.
- **Education:** informing the public and training communities.
- **Accountability:** exposing misconduct and pressuring institutions to follow their commitments.
- **Service delivery:** implementing development, health, education, and relief programs.
- **Agenda-setting:** bringing neglected issues into global debates.

### Influence Without Formal State Power

NGOs usually cannot sign treaties or vote in the UN General Assembly. Yet they can still influence global governance. They may provide evidence to international bodies, advise negotiators, mobilize public opinion, shape media narratives, organize campaigns, and pressure governments.

Some NGOs gain access to UN processes through consultative status with ECOSOC. This allows them to participate in certain meetings, submit statements, and contribute expertise. Civil society participation helps connect global institutions with communities, but access is not equal for all groups.

### Examples of NGO Work

Humanitarian NGOs may respond to disasters, conflicts, refugee crises, and health emergencies. Human rights organizations may document torture, unlawful detention, censorship, discrimination, or war crimes. Environmental NGOs may campaign for climate action, biodiversity protection, and pollution control. Development NGOs may work on education, food security, water, sanitation, gender equality, and poverty reduction.

### Strengths of NGOs and Civil Society

NGOs can have several strengths:

- closeness to local communities
- flexibility and speed
- specialized expertise
- moral authority
- ability to mobilize public pressure
- ability to monitor government behavior
- willingness to raise sensitive issues

### Limitations and Criticisms

NGOs also face criticisms and limits:

- they may depend on donors and external funding
- they may not represent everyone they claim to speak for
- they may have political biases
- they may be restricted or expelled by governments
- they may lack transparency or accountability
- they may compete with each other for funding
- they may be stronger in wealthy countries than in poorer ones

Civil society is therefore important but not automatically perfect. Its legitimacy depends on transparency, accountability, evidence, and connection to affected communities.

## Multinational Corporations and the Private Sector

Multinational corporations are companies that operate in more than one country. They are major actors in global governance because they shape trade, investment, technology, labor, supply chains, infrastructure, data, finance, energy, agriculture, pharmaceuticals, and communication.

The private sector can influence global governance through production, innovation, lobbying, standards, investment decisions, partnerships, and market power. Some corporations have revenues larger than the economies of many states, which gives them significant influence.

### How Corporations Influence Global Governance

Corporations influence global governance in several ways.

They shape **global supply chains**. A company’s sourcing decisions can affect labor conditions, environmental standards, and economic opportunities across many countries.

They shape **technology governance**. Technology companies affect digital privacy, artificial intelligence, cybersecurity, online speech, data flows, and platform regulation.

They shape **climate governance**. Energy companies, manufacturers, banks, insurers, and transport companies affect emissions, investment patterns, and green transition policies.

They shape **labor governance**. Corporate labor practices influence wages, working conditions, union rights, safety, and migration.

They shape **development**. Private investment can create jobs, infrastructure, and services, but it can also create dependency, displacement, or inequality.

They shape **standards**. Industry standards, certification systems, corporate codes of conduct, and reporting frameworks can influence behavior across borders.

### Public-Private Partnerships

Public-private partnerships involve cooperation between governments, international organizations, and private companies. They may support infrastructure, health programs, technology projects, climate finance, education, or humanitarian logistics.

Such partnerships can mobilize resources and expertise. For example, companies may provide technology, distribution systems, research capacity, or investment that governments alone cannot provide.

However, public-private partnerships require careful oversight. Private actors may prioritize profit. Public institutions must ensure that public interest, equity, transparency, and accountability are protected.

### Corporate Responsibility and the UN Global Compact

The UN Global Compact promotes principles-based corporate sustainability. Its principles cover human rights, labor, environment, and anti-corruption. It reflects an important idea: businesses are not only economic actors; they also have responsibilities in society.

Corporate sustainability initiatives can encourage better conduct, but voluntary commitments may be weak if they are not monitored or enforced. Some companies may use sustainability language for reputation while continuing harmful practices.

### Risks and Criticisms

Corporate influence can create risks:

- profit may conflict with human rights or environmental protection
- lobbying may weaken regulation
- companies may shift profits to reduce taxes
- workers may face exploitation in supply chains
- environmental damage may be externalized
- corporations may influence policy without democratic accountability
- digital companies may control data and communication spaces
- private actors may gain too much influence in public governance

The private sector is therefore both a necessary partner and a source of governance challenges.

## Expert Networks, Scientists, and Knowledge Communities

Expert networks are groups of specialists who provide knowledge for global decision-making. They include scientists, economists, lawyers, engineers, public health experts, climate researchers, technical standard-setters, policy analysts, and professional associations.

Global governance often depends on expert knowledge because many global problems are technically complex. Climate change, pandemics, artificial intelligence, nuclear safety, biodiversity loss, financial regulation, food security, and maritime law all require specialized understanding.

### Epistemic Communities

An epistemic community is a network of experts with recognized knowledge in a particular issue area. These experts may share professional standards, causal beliefs, and policy ideas. They influence global governance by defining problems, producing evidence, advising policymakers, and shaping what solutions appear credible.

For example, climate scientists helped establish the scientific basis for climate policy. Public health experts shape disease surveillance and emergency responses. Legal experts interpret treaties and advise states on dispute settlement. Economists influence development and financial governance.

### The IPCC as an Example

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change provides regular scientific assessments of climate change, its impacts, future risks, and options for adaptation and mitigation. The IPCC does not make climate policy by itself. Instead, it assesses scientific knowledge so policymakers have a common evidence base.

This shows how expert bodies can influence governance without being governments. They shape what is known, what is considered urgent, and what policy options are scientifically supported.

### Technical Standard-Setters

Technical standards are essential for global systems. Aviation, shipping, telecommunications, internet protocols, food safety, banking, product safety, and environmental measurement depend on standards. Experts help create these standards, often through specialized bodies or professional networks.

Standards may seem technical, but they can have political and economic consequences. A standard can determine who gains market access, what counts as safe, what data is collected, and which technologies become dominant.

### Limitations of Expertise

Expert influence has limits:

- evidence may be uncertain or incomplete
- experts may disagree
- technical advice may be ignored by political leaders
- scientific knowledge may be politicized
- poorer countries may have less access to expert networks
- experts may lack democratic accountability
- public distrust can weaken evidence-based policy

Expert knowledge is necessary, but it does not replace political judgment. Global governance requires both knowledge and legitimacy.

## Philanthropic Foundations

Philanthropic foundations are private organizations that use donated or endowed resources to fund public-interest activities. They can support global health, education, development, research, humanitarian assistance, climate action, democracy, gender equality, and poverty reduction.

Large foundations matter because they can move money quickly, support innovation, fund research, and influence agendas. They may work with governments, UN agencies, NGOs, universities, and private companies.

### Roles of Foundations

Foundations can:

- fund research and innovation
- support health and education programs
- finance pilot projects
- strengthen civil society organizations
- support advocacy campaigns
- provide grants to international organizations
- respond to crises
- support data, evaluation, and policy research

The Gates Foundation, for example, describes itself as a nonprofit working to fight poverty, disease, and inequity around the world. OECD work on philanthropy shows that private philanthropy contributes significant resources to development, though it remains smaller than official development assistance.

### Benefits

Foundations can provide flexible funding. They may support neglected problems that governments underfund. They can take risks on innovation and long-term research. They can also connect actors across sectors.

### Criticisms and Risks

Philanthropic foundations raise concerns about accountability. A foundation may influence public priorities without being elected. Donors may shape agendas according to personal values or institutional preferences. Foundation funding may encourage dependence among NGOs or international organizations. There may also be transparency concerns about how decisions are made.

Foundations can help global governance, but their power should be examined carefully. They are important actors, not neutral background donors.

## Social Movements and Advocacy Networks

Social movements and advocacy networks influence global governance from outside formal institutions. They are groups of people, organizations, and communities that mobilize around issues such as climate justice, human rights, labor rights, women’s rights, indigenous rights, anti-corruption, peace, racial justice, democracy, and environmental protection.

Unlike states or international organizations, social movements may not have formal authority. Their power often comes from public pressure, moral claims, visibility, storytelling, protest, organizing, and transnational solidarity.

### Transnational Advocacy Networks

Transnational advocacy networks are networks of activists, organizations, and allies that work across borders to influence policy. Academic work by Margaret Keck and Kathryn Sikkink emphasizes how such networks use information, symbols, leverage, and accountability politics to shape debates and pressure stronger actors.

These networks often help bring attention to issues that powerful actors ignore. They may connect local struggles with international forums. For example, a local human rights violation can become part of a global campaign if activists connect with NGOs, media, UN bodies, and foreign governments.

### Tools Used by Social Movements

Social movements use many tools:

- protests and demonstrations
- petitions and campaigns
- social media mobilization
- public education
- boycotts
- legal advocacy
- international solidarity networks
- media storytelling
- testimony from affected communities
- pressure on governments and corporations

### Influence on Global Governance

Social movements can change global agendas. They can pressure states to negotiate treaties, strengthen human rights standards, adopt climate targets, regulate corporations, or investigate abuses. They can also challenge the legitimacy of institutions that are seen as unjust or unresponsive.

### Limitations

Social movements face limits:

- they may be fragmented
- they may lack funding
- activists may face repression
- media attention may be temporary
- movements may disagree internally
- translating protest into policy can be difficult
- powerful actors may ignore or co-opt movement demands

Social movements do not always win, but they are important because they make global governance more responsive to public concerns and affected communities.

## Local Governments, Cities, and Subnational Actors

Local governments, cities, provinces, and regions are increasingly important in global governance. Many global problems are experienced locally. Climate change affects cities through heat, flooding, transport, housing, and energy use. Migration affects local schools, housing, jobs, and services. Public health emergencies require local clinics, hospitals, testing, communication, and community trust.

Global agreements often depend on local implementation. A national government may sign a climate agreement, but cities may manage public transport, building codes, waste systems, disaster response, and urban planning. Local governments turn global goals into practical action.

### Cities in Climate Governance

Cities are especially important in climate governance because they are centers of population, energy use, transport, infrastructure, and economic activity. City networks such as C40 connect mayors and urban governments to share strategies, data, finance pathways, and advocacy.

Cities can act faster than national governments in some areas. They can improve public transport, reduce emissions, create green spaces, prepare for floods, improve air quality, and regulate buildings. They can also influence national and global agendas by demonstrating practical solutions.

### Other Subnational Actors

Subnational governments include provinces, states, regions, and municipalities. In federal systems, these governments may control important policy areas such as education, environment, policing, health, infrastructure, and economic development.

### Limitations

Local governments face constraints:

- limited budgets
- limited legal authority
- dependence on national governments
- unequal capacity among cities
- political conflict across levels of government
- difficulty influencing formal international negotiations

Local actors are not replacements for states, but they are essential for implementation. Global governance often succeeds or fails at the local level.

## Individuals, Leaders, and Public Opinion

Individuals influence global governance in many ways. Political leaders make decisions, diplomats negotiate agreements, judges interpret law, activists mobilize campaigns, scientists produce knowledge, journalists investigate abuses, and citizens vote, protest, donate, share information, or pressure institutions.

### Political Leaders

Leaders can support or weaken international cooperation. A leader may champion climate action, negotiate peace, strengthen alliances, support human rights, or fund international organizations. Another leader may withdraw from agreements, reject court decisions, attack multilateralism, or prioritize narrow nationalism.

Leadership matters because global governance often depends on political will. Institutions may exist, but leaders decide whether to use them constructively.

### Diplomats

Diplomats are professional representatives of states. They negotiate treaties, build coalitions, explain national positions, mediate disputes, and maintain communication. Much global governance happens through diplomatic work that is slow, technical, and often invisible to the public.

### Activists and Public Figures

Activists can bring moral urgency to global problems. Public figures can draw attention to neglected issues. Survivors, community leaders, indigenous representatives, youth activists, and humanitarian workers may change how problems are understood.

### Journalists and Media

Journalists influence global governance by investigating abuses, explaining crises, exposing corruption, and shaping public attention. Media can pressure governments and institutions to respond. However, misinformation and propaganda can also distort public understanding.

### Citizens and Public Opinion

Citizens influence global governance through elections, public opinion, protest, consumer behavior, community organizing, and participation in civil society. Governments may be more or less willing to cooperate internationally depending on domestic public support.

Public opinion can support international cooperation, but it can also weaken it. Nationalism, polarization, misinformation, fear, and economic insecurity can make voters suspicious of international institutions.

## Contested or Harmful Actors

Not all actors in global governance contribute positively. Some actors create insecurity, undermine law, exploit weak institutions, spread disinformation, or profit from instability. These actors still matter because they force states and institutions to respond.

### Armed Groups

Armed groups can affect peace, humanitarian access, migration, human rights, and state stability. In civil wars or fragile states, armed groups may control territory, tax populations, recruit fighters, obstruct aid, or negotiate with governments and international mediators.

Humanitarian organizations may need to engage with armed groups to deliver aid or protect civilians, but this is difficult and politically sensitive.

### Terrorist Organizations

Terrorist organizations use violence against civilians or governments to pursue political, ideological, or religious goals. They affect global governance by creating security threats, cross-border cooperation, sanctions regimes, intelligence sharing, counterterrorism laws, and human rights debates.

Responses to terrorism can also create governance challenges. States must balance security with civil liberties, humanitarian law, and protection against discrimination.

### Criminal Networks and Illicit Markets

Transnational criminal networks operate across borders. They may be involved in drug trafficking, human trafficking, weapons smuggling, cybercrime, illegal fishing, money laundering, corruption, environmental crime, and illicit finance.

These networks weaken global governance by exploiting gaps between national legal systems. They require cooperation among police, customs agencies, financial regulators, courts, international organizations, and local communities.

### Cyber Actors

Cyber actors include states, criminal groups, private hackers, and politically motivated networks. They can attack infrastructure, steal data, spread disinformation, disrupt elections, and threaten financial systems.

Cyber governance is difficult because attacks can cross borders instantly, attribution is complex, and laws are still developing.

### Spoilers

Spoilers are actors that undermine peace processes, reforms, or cooperation because they benefit from conflict or instability. They may include armed factions, corrupt elites, extremist groups, or political actors who fear losing power.

Contested or harmful actors show why global governance is not only about cooperation. It also involves managing threats, preventing abuse, reducing harm, and protecting civilians.

## How Actors Work Together

Global governance often works through cooperation among many actors. No single actor usually has all the authority, information, money, legitimacy, and implementation capacity needed to solve global problems.

### Climate Change

Climate governance involves states negotiating agreements, scientists assessing evidence, cities reducing emissions, companies changing energy systems, financial institutions funding transitions, NGOs monitoring commitments, courts hearing climate cases, and citizens demanding action.

The Paris Agreement, national climate plans, IPCC assessments, climate finance, city networks, corporate sustainability commitments, and climate movements are all part of the wider climate governance system.

### Pandemics and Public Health

Health governance involves national health ministries, WHO, hospitals, laboratories, pharmaceutical companies, researchers, local governments, humanitarian organizations, border agencies, media, and citizens.

A pandemic requires surveillance, data sharing, testing, vaccine research, medical supply chains, public communication, emergency funding, and local implementation.

### Trade

Trade governance involves states, WTO institutions, customs agencies, corporations, workers, consumers, courts, regional trade agreements, and civil society. Trade rules shape markets, but businesses and workers experience the effects directly.

### Human Rights

Human rights governance involves states, UN bodies, courts, NGOs, journalists, activists, victims’ groups, regional organizations, and public opinion. States create and implement human rights obligations, but civil society often monitors violations and pressures governments.

### Maritime and Territorial Disputes

Maritime disputes involve coastal states, naval forces, international law, arbitration bodies, regional organizations, fishing communities, energy companies, shipping interests, and major powers. These disputes show how legal rules, state power, economic interests, and regional diplomacy interact.

### Humanitarian Crises

Humanitarian response involves affected states, UN agencies, NGOs, donors, local communities, armed actors, logistics companies, health workers, and security forces. Cooperation is essential, but conflict, access restrictions, funding shortages, and political manipulation can block aid.

## Power, Legitimacy, and Accountability

Different actors have different sources of power. Understanding these sources helps explain why some actors are influential even without formal authority.

### Legal Authority

States, courts, and treaty bodies have legal authority. They can create, interpret, or apply rules. Legal authority gives governance structure, but it depends on recognition and compliance.

### Military Power

States and alliances may possess military power. Military power can deter threats, protect territory, support peacekeeping, or coerce other actors. However, military power can also escalate conflict and undermine legitimacy if used unlawfully.

### Economic Power

States, corporations, financial institutions, and donors may use economic power. Loans, investments, sanctions, trade access, aid, and market size can influence behavior.

### Technical Expertise

Experts, agencies, scientists, and professional bodies influence decisions by defining problems and evaluating solutions. Technical expertise is powerful when policymakers trust it.

### Moral Authority

NGOs, humanitarian workers, victims’ groups, activists, religious leaders, and social movements may have moral authority. They can pressure powerful actors by appealing to justice, dignity, rights, or human suffering.

### Public Legitimacy

Legitimacy means that people or states view an actor’s authority as acceptable or justified. International institutions need legitimacy to maintain trust. If actors are seen as unfair, biased, secretive, or dominated by powerful interests, their legitimacy may weaken.

### Financial Resources

Money allows actors to fund programs, hire experts, support research, provide aid, or influence agendas. Wealthy states, corporations, foundations, and financial institutions often have significant influence because they control resources.

### Agenda-Setting Power

Agenda-setting power is the ability to decide what issues receive attention. The media, powerful states, UN leaders, NGOs, social movements, experts, and foundations can all shape agendas.

### Accountability

Accountability means actors can be questioned, monitored, corrected, or held responsible for their actions. Accountability is important because global governance often affects people who do not directly control global institutions.

Accountability problems include:

- unequal representation
- weak transparency
- donor influence
- corporate lobbying
- limited access for marginalized communities
- weak enforcement of rules
- lack of democratic control over some global institutions

Global governance must balance effectiveness with legitimacy. A system may act quickly but lack accountability, or it may be inclusive but slow. Good governance requires both capacity and trust.

## Why No Actor Can Govern Alone

No actor can govern alone because global problems are too complex and interconnected.

States are necessary, but they may lack expertise, trust, capacity, or willingness. International organizations provide coordination, but they depend on states. NGOs provide advocacy and services, but they need access and funding. Experts provide knowledge, but they need political uptake. Corporations provide innovation and resources, but they need regulation. Cities implement many policies, but they need national support. Courts interpret law, but they need jurisdiction and compliance. Citizens provide legitimacy, but they need accurate information and meaningful participation.

Global governance works best when actors complement one another:

- states provide authority and implementation
- international organizations provide coordination and legitimacy
- courts provide legal interpretation
- financial institutions provide resources
- NGOs provide advocacy, services, and monitoring
- experts provide evidence
- companies provide technology, investment, and supply-chain action
- foundations provide funding and innovation
- cities provide local implementation
- citizens and movements provide pressure and legitimacy

The weakness of global governance is that these actors do not always agree. The strength of global governance is that cooperation among many actors can make solutions possible even without a single world government.

## Key Terms and Simple Explanations

### State Actor

A state actor is a government or part of a government. Examples include national governments, ministries, diplomats, courts, militaries, and regulatory agencies.

### Non-State Actor

A non-state actor is an actor that is not a government. Examples include NGOs, corporations, foundations, social movements, expert networks, and media organizations.

### Intergovernmental Organization

An intergovernmental organization is an institution created by states through agreement. Examples include the United Nations, World Trade Organization, World Health Organization, IMF, and World Bank.

### International Organization

An international organization is an organization that operates across borders. It may be intergovernmental, like the UN, or non-governmental, like an international NGO.

### Regional Organization

A regional organization is an institution formed by states in a particular region. Examples include ASEAN, the European Union, African Union, Organization of American States, and Arab League.

### Civil Society

Civil society refers to organizations and groups outside the state and market that represent citizen interests, community concerns, advocacy, service, or public participation.

### NGO

An NGO is a non-governmental organization. NGOs may work on humanitarian aid, development, human rights, environment, health, education, or advocacy.

### Multinational Corporation

A multinational corporation is a company that operates in more than one country. It can influence global governance through investment, supply chains, technology, employment, and lobbying.

### International Financial Institution

An international financial institution provides loans, financial support, policy advice, or development finance across borders. Examples include the World Bank, IMF, and regional development banks.

### International Court

An international court is a legal body that helps interpret international law, resolve disputes, or support accountability. Examples include the ICJ and ICC.

### Multilateralism

Multilateralism is cooperation among three or more states, often through international institutions or agreements.

### Public-Private Partnership

A public-private partnership is cooperation between public authorities and private actors, usually to deliver services, finance projects, or solve policy problems.

### Transnational Actor

A transnational actor operates across borders and influences international issues. NGOs, companies, advocacy networks, and criminal networks can be transnational actors.

### Epistemic Community

An epistemic community is a network of experts with recognized knowledge in a specific area. They influence policy by providing evidence and shared understandings.

### Legitimacy

Legitimacy means that an actor or institution is seen as acceptable, justified, or trustworthy.

### Accountability

Accountability means that actors can be monitored, questioned, corrected, or held responsible for their decisions and actions.

### Enforcement Gap

An enforcement gap exists when rules or decisions exist but are difficult to enforce. This often happens in global governance because there is no single world government or global police force.

## Common Misconceptions

### “Only countries matter in global governance.”

Countries are central, but they are not the only actors. International organizations, NGOs, companies, experts, foundations, cities, courts, and citizens also influence global governance.

### “The United Nations controls all countries.”

The UN does not control all countries like a world government. It is an organization of member states. It can coordinate, recommend, authorize, monitor, and support action, but it depends heavily on state cooperation.

### “NGOs have no power because they are not governments.”

NGOs may lack formal state power, but they can influence global governance through advocacy, research, monitoring, humanitarian work, public pressure, and participation in international forums.

### “Corporations are not political actors.”

Corporations can be political actors because they influence regulation, labor standards, technology, investment, climate policy, and public policy through lobbying, markets, supply chains, and partnerships.

### “International courts can always force states to obey.”

International courts can clarify law and issue judgments, but they often depend on state consent and cooperation. Enforcement can be difficult, especially when powerful states resist.

### “Global governance is the same as world government.”

Global governance is not world government. It is a system of cooperation among many actors. There is no single global authority that governs all states.

### “Regional organizations are less important than global organizations.”

Regional organizations can be very important because they often understand local conditions and can respond to regional problems. They can also support or implement global goals.

### “Experts decide global policy.”

Experts influence policy by providing evidence and advice, but political actors usually make final decisions. Expertise is powerful, but it does not replace legitimacy or political choice.

### “Private foundations are only donors.”

Foundations are not only donors. They can shape research agendas, fund programs, influence partnerships, and affect policy priorities. Their influence can be useful but also raises accountability questions.

### “Public opinion does not matter internationally.”

Public opinion matters because governments respond to voters, protests, media pressure, and legitimacy concerns. Public support can strengthen or weaken international cooperation.
