# Topic 7: Enforcement Gap: Ruling vs Reality

## Short Overview

The enforcement gap is one of the most important ideas in global governance. It explains why international rules, treaties, legal judgments, and arbitral awards can be meaningful but still difficult to implement in practice.

In domestic law, a court decision is usually backed by a state’s police, courts, prison system, tax authority, and centralized government. In international law, enforcement is different. There is no single world government, no universal global police force, and no central authority that can automatically force every state to obey every legal ruling. Instead, international enforcement depends on a mix of legal obligation, state consent, reputation, diplomacy, pressure, sanctions, incentives, domestic politics, international institutions, and power relations.

This topic focuses on the gap between **what the law says** and **what actually happens**. A legal ruling can clarify rights and obligations, but it may not automatically change behavior on the ground or at sea. This is especially clear in the West Philippine Sea / South China Sea case. The 2016 arbitral award clarified important legal issues under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea, or UNCLOS, but China rejected the award and continued to maintain its position and presence in the South China Sea.

This does not mean that international law is useless. It means that international law often works through long-term legitimacy, diplomacy, reputational pressure, coalition-building, and rule development rather than instant physical enforcement. A ruling can matter legally, diplomatically, and politically even when practical enforcement remains incomplete.

A simple student-friendly explanation is: **an enforcement gap happens when a rule or ruling exists, but reality does not fully follow it.**

## Core Definition: What Is an Enforcement Gap?

An enforcement gap happens when a rule, treaty, judgment, arbitral award, or international decision exists but is not fully implemented, obeyed, or enforced.

The enforcement gap is the distance between a formal rule and real-world behavior. It is the gap between legal authority and practical effect.

For example:

- A court issues a judgment, but the losing state refuses to follow it.
- A treaty is signed, but a government does not implement it domestically.
- A climate promise is announced, but actual emissions do not fall enough.
- A human rights rule exists, but abuses continue.
- A maritime ruling clarifies legal rights, but vessels continue to act as if the ruling did not exist.
- A ceasefire agreement is signed, but fighting continues.
- An international arrest warrant is issued, but the suspect is not arrested because states do not cooperate.

The enforcement gap is not limited to one country, one organization, or one court. It is a structural problem in global governance because international law operates in a decentralized system of sovereign states.

The enforcement gap is especially important for students of global governance because it helps explain why global problems are difficult to solve. Global governance can create rules, procedures, and decisions, but those tools still need political will, cooperation, resources, and implementation.

## Enforcement vs Compliance

Enforcement and compliance are related, but they are not the same.

### Compliance

Compliance means that an actor follows a rule, obligation, treaty, judgment, or decision. A state complies when its behavior matches what the rule requires.

For example, a state complies with a treaty when it changes its domestic laws to meet treaty obligations. A state complies with an arbitral award when it follows the ruling and changes behavior accordingly.

### Enforcement

Enforcement means making a rule or decision effective. Enforcement includes the mechanisms, pressures, institutions, or actions that encourage or compel compliance.

Enforcement can include:

- court orders
- sanctions
- diplomatic pressure
- reputational costs
- monitoring
- reporting
- economic consequences
- domestic legal action
- international organization action
- collective pressure by other states
- incentives and assistance
- military deterrence in some security contexts

A simple distinction is:

- **Compliance** is following the rule.
- **Enforcement** is making the rule effective.

In global governance, compliance often happens without direct enforcement. States may comply because they want a good reputation, want reciprocal cooperation, want economic benefits, or believe the law is legitimate. But when a state refuses to comply, enforcement becomes more difficult because there may be no central authority to force obedience.

## International Law vs Domestic Law

International law does not work exactly like domestic law.

### Domestic Law

Domestic law operates inside a state. A national government usually has centralized authority. It can pass laws, collect taxes, run courts, punish violations, and use police powers. If a person violates a domestic court order, the state can usually enforce the order through police, fines, prison, or other legal mechanisms.

Domestic legal systems usually have:

- legislatures
- courts
- police
- prosecutors
- prisons
- tax authorities
- administrative agencies
- centralized executive power

### International Law

International law operates among states and other international actors. It has courts, treaties, organizations, and rules, but it does not have a single global government above all states.

International law usually depends on:

- state consent
- treaties
- customary rules
- international organizations
- diplomatic pressure
- reputation
- reciprocity
- domestic implementation
- sanctions
- monitoring
- peer pressure
- collective action

There is no world police force that automatically enforces every international judgment. There is no world executive government that can directly compel every state to comply. This makes enforcement more political and uneven.

This does not mean international law is not law. It means that international law is enforced differently. It often works through legitimacy, expectation, coordination, institutions, and long-term pressure rather than immediate coercion.

## Why Enforcement Is Difficult in Global Governance

Enforcement is difficult in global governance for several structural reasons.

### State Sovereignty

States are sovereign. They have authority over their territory and political decisions. International institutions often cannot act inside a state or against a state without legal authority, consent, or political support.

Sovereignty makes enforcement difficult because states may reject outside pressure if they believe it threatens national independence, security, or identity.

### Lack of World Police

There is no single global police force with automatic jurisdiction over all states. International institutions may monitor or issue decisions, but they often cannot physically compel compliance.

### Dependence on State Consent

Many courts and tribunals depend on state consent or treaty-based jurisdiction. Even when a state has accepted legal jurisdiction, later enforcement may still depend on cooperation.

### Unequal Power

Powerful states may be harder to pressure because they have military strength, economic resources, diplomatic influence, allies, and institutional privileges. Weaker states may face consequences more easily than powerful states.

### National Interest

States may resist rules or rulings when they believe compliance would harm national interest. A state may prioritize security, territory, domestic politics, economic interests, or strategic advantage over legal compliance.

### Weak Penalties

Some international rules have weak penalties. If non-compliance has few costs, a state may choose to ignore the rule.

### Limited Jurisdiction

A court or tribunal can only decide issues within its jurisdiction. It may not be able to decide every part of a dispute or enforce every consequence.

### Lack of Political Will

Enforcement often requires other states or institutions to act. If they do not want to risk confrontation, economic costs, or diplomatic conflict, enforcement may be weak.

### Institutional Weakness

International organizations may lack funding, staff, authority, or enforcement tools. They may issue reports or recommendations but lack the power to force compliance.

### Funding and Capacity Limits

Even willing states may fail to comply if they lack money, administrative capacity, trained personnel, legal institutions, or technical expertise.

### Geopolitical Rivalry

Major-power rivalry can block enforcement. If powerful states disagree, institutions such as the United Nations Security Council may be unable to act.

These factors create a gap between legal rules and actual behavior. This is why global governance often relies on gradual pressure, negotiation, and legitimacy rather than instant enforcement.

## Sovereignty and Non-Compliance

Sovereignty is the authority of a state to govern itself and control its territory, people, and political decisions. Sovereignty is central to international law, but it also creates enforcement challenges.

A state may reject outside authority if it believes an international ruling threatens national security, territorial claims, domestic legitimacy, or strategic interests. This does not mean sovereignty gives states permission to ignore all law. States can and do have international obligations. But sovereignty means enforcement against a state is usually more difficult than enforcement against an individual inside a national legal system.

### Why States Sometimes Comply

States may comply with international rulings because:

- they accepted the legal process
- they value their reputation
- they expect reciprocity
- they want stable diplomatic relations
- they want investment or economic benefits
- they face domestic legal pressure
- they want to appear law-abiding
- they fear sanctions or retaliation
- they believe compliance supports long-term interests

### Why States Sometimes Resist

States may resist international rulings because:

- they reject the court or tribunal’s jurisdiction
- the ruling threatens territory or resources
- domestic nationalism makes compromise costly
- leaders fear appearing weak
- powerful allies provide protection
- enforcement costs are low
- the state believes the ruling is unfair
- geopolitical interests outweigh legal costs
- the state has enough power to absorb criticism

Sovereignty makes enforcement a political process. International law creates obligations, but state behavior depends on incentives, pressure, power, and legitimacy.

## Power Politics and Unequal Enforcement

Global governance does not operate in a world of equal power. States may be legally equal in many institutions, but they are not equal in military strength, economic resources, diplomatic reach, technological capacity, or institutional influence.

Power affects enforcement in several ways.

### Powerful States Are Harder to Pressure

Powerful states may resist enforcement because they can absorb diplomatic criticism, use economic leverage, rely on allies, or block action in international institutions. If a powerful state has a permanent seat on the UN Security Council, veto politics may further limit enforcement options.

### Weaker States May Depend on Law

Smaller or weaker states often rely on international law because they cannot easily use military or economic power against stronger states. Legal processes can help them clarify rights, gain legitimacy, attract support, and frame disputes in rule-based terms.

### Unequal Enforcement

Unequal power can produce selective enforcement. Rules may be enforced strongly against weaker states but weakly against powerful states. This creates accusations of double standards and can weaken trust in global governance.

Power politics does not erase international law, but it affects how law is applied and enforced. This is why students should understand both law and power.

## Legal Victory vs Practical Enforcement

Legal victory and practical enforcement are different.

### Legal Victory

A legal victory happens when a court, tribunal, or legal process rules in favor of a state or party. Legal victory may mean:

- a tribunal clarifies rights
- a claim is rejected
- a state’s legal position is strengthened
- a treaty interpretation is confirmed
- a state gains international legitimacy
- an authoritative legal record is created

Legal victory is important because it can shape diplomacy, public opinion, future negotiations, and international norms.

### Practical Enforcement

Practical enforcement means changing real-world behavior. It may require:

- withdrawal from an area
- access restored to a fishing ground or resource area
- compensation paid
- domestic laws changed
- military or coast guard behavior altered
- environmental harm stopped
- sanctions imposed
- international monitoring
- diplomatic pressure
- alliance support
- public mobilization
- long-term negotiation

A state may win legally but still struggle to enforce the outcome. This does not make the legal victory meaningless. It means the victory must be translated into political, diplomatic, and practical action.

A simple explanation is: **legal victory tells the world what the law says; practical enforcement determines whether behavior actually changes.**

## Why Legal Rulings Still Matter

Legal rulings matter even when enforcement is difficult. They can have important effects beyond immediate compliance.

### Legal Clarification

Rulings clarify rights and obligations. They explain what the law requires and what claims are legally valid or invalid.

### International Legitimacy

A ruling can strengthen a state’s legitimacy. It allows the state to say that its position is grounded in law, not only national interest.

### Diplomatic Leverage

A state can cite a ruling in diplomatic protests, speeches, negotiations, and international forums.

### Reputational Pressure

A state that rejects a ruling may face reputational costs. Other states, organizations, and publics may view non-compliance as a violation of legal order.

### Coalition-Building

Legal rulings can help states build coalitions. Other states may support a rule-based position even if they are not direct parties to the dispute.

### Public Awareness

A ruling can inform citizens, media, students, and civil society about legal rights and obligations.

### Evidence for Future Negotiations

Legal rulings create records that can influence future diplomacy, negotiations, and agreements.

### Strengthening Rules-Based Order

A ruling can reinforce the idea that disputes should be handled through law rather than force.

### Long-Term Norm Development

Even if a ruling is not immediately enforced, it may influence future behavior and legal interpretation over time.

The value of legal rulings should not be measured only by immediate compliance. International law often shapes politics gradually.

## Binding Decisions and the Problem of Enforcement

A binding decision is a decision that parties have a legal obligation to follow. Binding does not mean automatically enforced.

This is a common source of confusion. A student might assume that if a ruling is legally binding, then someone will immediately force the losing party to obey. In international law, this is often not true.

A binding decision may still face enforcement problems if:

- the losing state rejects the ruling
- there is no direct enforcement mechanism
- other states avoid confrontation
- a powerful state blocks collective action
- domestic politics prevent compliance
- the ruling requires long-term implementation
- the institution lacks coercive power

Examples of binding decisions include:

- judgments in certain international court cases
- arbitral awards
- treaty obligations
- some Security Council decisions

The key point is: **binding creates legal obligation; enforcement creates practical effect.**

## UNCLOS and Final Binding Awards

UNCLOS includes a dispute settlement system in Part XV. Article 296 states that decisions rendered by a court or tribunal with jurisdiction under the relevant section are final and shall be complied with by the parties to the dispute. It also states that the decision has no binding force except between the parties and in respect of that particular dispute.

This is important for the 2016 South China Sea arbitration because the award was issued by an arbitral tribunal constituted under Annex VII of UNCLOS. The tribunal considered whether it had jurisdiction and issued a final award.

The legal point is that UNCLOS creates a framework for final and binding dispute settlement decisions. The practical point is that a final and binding award may still be difficult to enforce if one party rejects the decision.

A careful student explanation is: **UNCLOS can make an award legally binding on the parties, but UNCLOS does not create a world police force to enforce that award physically.**

## Case Study Introduction: The 2016 South China Sea Arbitral Award

The 2016 South China Sea arbitral award is the main case example for understanding ruling versus reality.

The Philippines brought the case against China under UNCLOS dispute settlement procedures. The arbitral tribunal was constituted under Annex VII of UNCLOS. The Permanent Court of Arbitration served as registry, meaning it provided administrative support for the proceedings. The award was issued on 12 July 2016.

The arbitration concerned legal issues such as:

- the role of historic rights in the South China Sea
- the source of maritime entitlements
- the status of certain maritime features
- whether certain features could generate maritime zones
- the lawfulness of certain Chinese activities under UNCLOS
- environmental harm
- interference with the Philippines’ sovereign rights

China did not participate in the proceedings and rejected the award. The tribunal continued under UNCLOS procedures and issued its ruling.

The award clarified important legal issues, but it did not automatically change conditions at sea. This is why the case is a strong example of the enforcement gap.

## What the 2016 Award Clarified

The 2016 award clarified several important legal issues.

### Historic Rights and the Nine-Dash Line

The tribunal found that China had no legal basis to claim historic rights to resources within the nine-dash line where those rights exceeded the maritime zones provided by UNCLOS. This was one of the most important findings because it challenged the legal basis of broad historic-rights claims.

### Maritime Entitlements

The tribunal explained that maritime entitlements in the South China Sea must be based on UNCLOS. Maritime rights come from land territory and maritime features as defined by the Convention, not from vague claims to large sea areas.

### Status of Features

The tribunal classified certain features as rocks, low-tide elevations, or submerged features. These classifications mattered because different features generate different maritime zones.

### Exclusive Economic Zone Issues

The tribunal found that certain areas were within the Philippines’ exclusive economic zone and continental shelf, and that China had violated the Philippines’ sovereign rights through certain activities.

### Environmental Harm

The tribunal found that China’s large-scale land reclamation and construction activities caused severe harm to coral reef environments and violated obligations to protect and preserve the marine environment.

### Certain Chinese Activities

The tribunal found that some Chinese activities interfered with Philippine fishing and petroleum exploration, and that certain law enforcement vessel conduct created serious risk of collision.

For Topic 7, the key point is not to repeat every legal detail from Topic 6. The key point is that the tribunal clarified law, but enforcement remained difficult.

## What the 2016 Award Did Not Do

The 2016 award did not solve every part of the South China Sea dispute.

### It Did Not Decide Sovereignty Over Land Features

The tribunal did not decide which state owns islands, rocks, or other land features. Sovereignty over land territory was outside the scope of the decision.

### It Did Not Delimit a Maritime Boundary

The tribunal did not draw a maritime boundary between the Philippines and China.

### It Did Not Give the Philippines Sovereignty Over the Entire South China Sea

The award clarified specific rights and obligations under UNCLOS. It did not give the Philippines ownership of the entire South China Sea.

### It Did Not Create a Global Police Force

The tribunal did not have enforcement forces. It could issue a legal award, but it could not physically compel compliance.

### It Did Not Automatically End the Dispute

The award was legally significant, but it did not automatically stop maritime tensions, competing claims, or incidents at sea.

This distinction matters because overclaiming the award can make the chatbot inaccurate. The award was powerful legally, but limited in practical enforcement.

## China’s Rejection of the Award

China rejected the arbitration and the 2016 award. China’s official position is that the tribunal lacked jurisdiction, that the arbitration was invalid, and that the award is null and void. China has stated that it does not accept or recognize the award.

China’s rejection created a practical enforcement problem. If a state refuses to comply with an award, the tribunal does not have a direct police force to enforce compliance. Other states may criticize, issue statements, conduct operations, support the winning party diplomatically, or apply pressure. But these actions depend on politics.

China’s rejection illustrates an important global governance issue: **legal obligation does not always produce actual compliance when a powerful state rejects the authority or outcome of a legal process.**

## The Philippines’ Use of the Award

The Philippines uses the 2016 award as a legal and diplomatic tool.

### Diplomatic Protests

The Philippines can cite the award in diplomatic protests when it believes Chinese actions violate Philippine rights under UNCLOS.

### Public Communication

The award helps the Philippine government explain its position to citizens, students, media, and the international community.

### International Support

Other states may support the award or refer to it when discussing the rules-based order, freedom of navigation, and UNCLOS.

### Alliance-Building

The award strengthens the Philippines’ law-based position as it works with allies and partners on maritime security, coast guard capacity, and diplomatic support.

### Law-Based Framing

The Philippines can frame the dispute as an issue of international law rather than only as a bilateral power struggle.

### Defense of Sovereign Rights

The award supports the Philippines’ argument that it has sovereign rights in parts of its exclusive economic zone and continental shelf.

The award strengthened the Philippines’ position because it provided legal clarity and legitimacy. However, enforcement still requires diplomacy, maritime presence, public pressure, partnerships, and long-term strategy.

## Ruling vs Reality in the West Philippine Sea

The phrase “ruling vs reality” describes the difference between the legal award and continuing conditions at sea.

### The Ruling

The 2016 award clarified that certain Chinese claims and activities were inconsistent with UNCLOS. It supported important Philippine legal positions. It rejected historic rights claims within the nine-dash line where they exceeded UNCLOS entitlements.

### The Reality

Despite the ruling, maritime tensions continued. Issues include:

- continuing Chinese presence in disputed areas
- coast guard confrontations
- gray-zone activities
- blocking or shadowing of vessels
- water cannon incidents
- resupply tensions at Second Thomas Shoal / Ayungin Shoal
- fisherfolk access issues
- diplomatic protests
- cautious regional responses
- slow ASEAN-China Code of Conduct negotiations

These realities show the enforcement gap. The legal ruling clarified rights, but it did not automatically change behavior.

## Gray-Zone Activities and Enforcement Difficulty

Gray-zone activities are actions that are coercive but fall below the threshold of open armed conflict. They are designed to pressure another state without triggering full-scale war.

In maritime disputes, gray-zone activities may include:

- water cannon use
- blocking vessels
- shadowing vessels
- swarming by large numbers of boats
- maritime militia allegations
- coast guard pressure
- fishing restrictions
- radio challenges
- dangerous maneuvers
- interference with resupply missions
- occupation or construction activities
- administrative declarations

Gray-zone activities make enforcement harder because they blur categories. They may not look like traditional military attacks, but they can still change facts on the water. They may be presented as law enforcement or civilian activity, but they can have strategic effects.

This creates a challenge for global governance. If an action is below the threshold of war, states may hesitate to respond strongly. But if they do not respond, the activity may gradually change control, access, or expectations.

Gray-zone activity shows why enforcement is not only about courts and legal texts. It is also about presence, deterrence, diplomacy, and crisis management.

## Why the UN Cannot Simply Enforce the Award

A common misconception is that the United Nations can simply force China to obey the 2016 award. This is not accurate.

### The Award Came from UNCLOS Arbitration

The award was issued by an Annex VII arbitral tribunal under UNCLOS. It was not a Security Council enforcement order and not a judgment of the International Court of Justice.

### The UN Has No Standing Global Police Force

The UN does not have a standing global police force that can automatically enforce every international legal ruling.

### Security Council Action Is Political

The UN Security Council can take binding action in certain situations involving threats to international peace and security. But Security Council action is political and depends on the votes of its members.

### China Is a Permanent Member of the Security Council

China is one of the five permanent members of the Security Council. Permanent members have veto power over substantive decisions. This means any attempt to use the Security Council to enforce an award against China would face major political obstacles.

### Enforcement Through the UN Would Face Veto Politics

Because China can veto substantive Security Council action, binding enforcement through the Council would be extremely unlikely.

This does not mean the UN is useless. The UN and UNCLOS provide legal frameworks, forums, principles, and dispute settlement mechanisms. But they do not automatically enforce every ruling against every state.

## ASEAN and Regional Enforcement Limits

ASEAN is important for regional diplomacy, but it is not a strong enforcement mechanism for the 2016 award.

### ASEAN’s Role

ASEAN provides a regional forum for dialogue. It supports regional stability, peaceful settlement, restraint, and negotiations with China, including discussions toward a Code of Conduct in the South China Sea.

### Why ASEAN Has Difficulty Enforcing a Unified Position

ASEAN faces several limits:

- consensus decision-making
- different national interests
- some members are claimants while others are not
- different economic relationships with China
- different threat perceptions
- reluctance to escalate tensions
- limited enforcement authority
- slow Code of Conduct negotiations

ASEAN can support diplomacy, but it cannot easily force China or member states to comply with a ruling. Its value is mainly diplomatic and regional, not coercive.

## Diplomacy After Legal Rulings

After a legal ruling, diplomacy becomes essential. International law can clarify the legal position, but diplomacy helps translate that position into action.

Diplomacy after a ruling may involve:

- bilateral talks
- diplomatic protests
- ASEAN meetings
- United Nations statements
- alliance consultations
- joint statements with partners
- public diplomacy
- coast guard cooperation
- confidence-building measures
- negotiation of rules for behavior
- crisis communication mechanisms

A ruling often becomes part of a long diplomatic process. It can shape the language of negotiations and strengthen a party’s position, but it rarely removes the need for negotiation.

Diplomacy after a ruling does not mean the ruling failed. It means legal decisions often need diplomatic follow-through.

## Reputation, Legitimacy, and International Pressure

International law often works through reputation and legitimacy. States care about how they are seen by other states, investors, international organizations, domestic audiences, and global publics.

### Reputational Pressure

A state that rejects a ruling may face criticism. Other states may issue statements, support the winning party, or cite the ruling in international forums. Media, scholars, civil society groups, and legal experts may also reinforce the ruling’s importance.

### International Legitimacy

Legal rulings can affect legitimacy. A state that follows international law may appear more credible. A state that rejects legal rulings may face questions about its commitment to the rules-based order.

### Limits of Reputation

Reputation matters, but it may not be enough. A powerful state may decide that strategic gains outweigh reputational costs. Domestic audiences may reward defiance rather than compliance. Allies or economic partners may avoid strong criticism.

Reputational pressure is real but incomplete. It is one tool among many.

## Sanctions, Incentives, and Costs

States and institutions may try to encourage compliance through costs or incentives.

### Sanctions

Sanctions can include economic restrictions, travel bans, asset freezes, arms embargoes, or other penalties. In the South China Sea case, broad sanctions are difficult because many states have major economic ties with China and may not want escalation.

### Economic Costs

Economic consequences may influence state behavior, but they can also harm the states applying them. This makes enforcement politically difficult.

### Diplomatic Isolation

States may try to isolate a non-compliant actor diplomatically. But isolation is difficult when the actor is economically or strategically important.

### Incentives

Incentives may include cooperation, economic benefits, negotiations, or face-saving arrangements that encourage compliance without direct confrontation.

### Security Partnerships

States may build security partnerships to strengthen deterrence and support rights. This can include defense cooperation, coast guard training, intelligence sharing, and joint exercises.

### Capacity-Building

Capacity-building helps a state protect its rights more effectively. For the Philippines, this may include maritime domain awareness, coast guard modernization, legal capacity, and alliance cooperation.

### Why Tools Are Difficult to Apply

These tools are difficult in the South China Sea because of economic interdependence, risk of escalation, China’s power, ASEAN divisions, and the complexity of maritime incidents.

## Deterrence and Maritime Presence

When legal rulings are difficult to enforce directly, states may try to protect their rights through deterrence and maritime presence.

### Maritime Presence

Maritime presence may include:

- coast guard patrols
- naval patrols
- resupply missions
- civilian fishing presence
- maritime surveillance
- aerial monitoring
- joint patrols
- public reporting of incidents

Presence can show that a state continues to assert its rights.

### Deterrence

Deterrence means discouraging another actor from taking unwanted action by increasing the expected costs or risks. Deterrence may involve alliances, defense commitments, coast guard capacity, or visible patrols.

### Benefits

Deterrence and presence can:

- protect access
- show resolve
- support legal claims
- reassure domestic audiences
- deter coercion
- gather evidence
- support alliances

### Risks

They can also create risks:

- escalation
- collisions
- military confrontation
- domestic pressure to respond strongly
- miscalculation
- worsening relations

Deterrence can protect rights, but it must be managed carefully to avoid turning legal disputes into armed conflict.

## Why Smaller States Use International Law

Smaller or weaker states often use international law because they cannot easily match the military or economic power of larger states.

International law helps smaller states by providing:

- legitimacy
- peaceful dispute settlement
- coalition-building
- legal clarity
- public support
- diplomatic leverage
- a way to challenge excessive claims
- a record for future negotiations
- a platform for international support

The Philippines is a major example. By bringing the arbitration, the Philippines used law to challenge a more powerful state. It could not force immediate compliance, but it gained legal clarity and international legitimacy.

This shows that international law can be a tool of weaker states, even when enforcement is difficult.

## Why Powerful States May Resist Rulings

Powerful states may resist adverse rulings for several reasons.

### National Interest

A state may believe the ruling harms its security, territory, resources, or strategic position.

### Strategic Advantage

A state may already control territory, waters, or infrastructure and may not want to give up advantages.

### Domestic Nationalism

Leaders may fear domestic backlash if they appear to accept an adverse ruling on sovereignty or maritime rights.

### Rejection of Jurisdiction

A state may argue that a court or tribunal had no authority to decide the case.

### Manageable Costs

A powerful state may believe the costs of non-compliance are manageable compared with the benefits of maintaining its position.

### Ability to Absorb Criticism

Powerful states may have enough economic and diplomatic influence to absorb criticism.

### Military or Economic Leverage

Military and economic strength may make other states cautious about applying pressure.

This does not mean powerful states never obey international law. Powerful states often use and comply with international law when it supports their interests. But when the law conflicts with high-priority strategic goals, they may resist.

## Enforcement Gap Beyond the South China Sea

Enforcement gaps are not unique to the South China Sea. They appear across global governance.

### Climate Commitments

States may make climate pledges but fail to implement policies strong enough to meet them.

### Human Rights Rulings

Human rights bodies or courts may issue findings, but governments may ignore them or delay implementation.

### Humanitarian Law

Rules protecting civilians may exist, but violations can continue during armed conflict.

### Trade Disputes

Trade rulings may be delayed, resisted, or affected by retaliation and negotiation.

### International Criminal Warrants

International criminal warrants may not be enforced if states refuse to arrest suspects.

These examples show that enforcement gaps are a general feature of global governance. The South China Sea case is a powerful example, but it is part of a broader pattern.

## Strengths of Global Governance Shown by the Enforcement Gap

The enforcement gap does not only show weakness. It also reveals what global governance can still do.

### Creates Rules

Global governance creates rules such as UNCLOS, which defines maritime zones and dispute settlement procedures.

### Provides Dispute Settlement Forums

International courts and tribunals give states peaceful legal options.

### Gives Smaller States Legal Tools

Legal mechanisms allow smaller states to challenge stronger states without using force.

### Produces Authoritative Records

Rulings create evidence, reasoning, and legal records that can shape future diplomacy.

### Supports Peaceful Legal Argument

Law provides a structured way to argue about rights and obligations.

### Creates Diplomatic Leverage

A ruling gives states a basis for protests, speeches, alliances, and negotiations.

### Shapes International Opinion

Legal findings can influence how other states and publics understand the dispute.

These strengths matter because global governance is often about building pressure and legitimacy over time.

## Limits of Global Governance Shown by the Enforcement Gap

The enforcement gap also shows major limits.

Global governance struggles to:

- force powerful states to comply
- prevent gray-zone pressure
- overcome veto politics
- unify regional actors
- translate legal rulings into immediate behavior
- protect affected communities quickly
- impose costs on major powers
- prevent escalation while defending rights
- ensure consistent enforcement

These limits are central to the project’s global governance theme. Global governance is important, but it operates in a world of sovereign states and unequal power.

## Balanced Evaluation

Students should avoid two extremes.

The first extreme is saying international law is useless because enforcement is difficult. This is wrong because law can clarify rights, shape legitimacy, support diplomacy, and strengthen weaker states.

The second extreme is saying legal rulings automatically solve disputes. This is also wrong because legal rulings still need compliance, enforcement, diplomacy, and political support.

A balanced view is: **legal rulings can be meaningful and limited at the same time.**

In the South China Sea case, the 2016 award clarified important legal issues and strengthened the Philippines’ position. But China’s rejection and continuing maritime tensions show that legal victory does not automatically create practical enforcement.

Global governance often works through long-term pressure, legal legitimacy, diplomacy, alliances, public opinion, and norm-building. It rarely works through instant enforcement.

## Common Misconceptions

### “If a ruling is binding, it is automatically enforced.”

Incorrect. Binding means the parties have a legal obligation to comply. It does not mean enforcement happens automatically.

### “If China rejects the award, the award has no legal importance.”

Incorrect. China’s rejection creates an enforcement problem, but the award still has legal and diplomatic significance.

### “The UN can simply force China to obey.”

Incorrect. The UN has no standing global police force, and Security Council enforcement would face veto politics because China is a permanent member.

### “International law is useless without world police.”

Incorrect. International law also works through legitimacy, reputation, diplomacy, domestic law, institutions, and long-term norm development.

### “The Philippines’ legal victory solved the dispute.”

Incorrect. The award clarified legal issues and strengthened the Philippines’ position, but tensions and enforcement problems continue.

### “The ruling gave the Philippines the whole South China Sea.”

Incorrect. The award did not decide sovereignty over land features and did not give the Philippines the entire sea.

### “Only military power matters.”

Incorrect. Military power matters, but law, diplomacy, public opinion, alliances, and legitimacy also shape outcomes.

### “Smaller states cannot use international law.”

Incorrect. Smaller states can use law to clarify rights, build coalitions, and challenge stronger states.

### “Diplomacy after a ruling means the ruling failed.”

Incorrect. Diplomacy is often necessary to implement and defend legal rulings.

### “Gray-zone activities are not important because they are not full war.”

Incorrect. Gray-zone activities can change conditions, create pressure, and undermine legal rights without triggering open conflict.

## Key Terms and Simple Explanations

### Enforcement

Actions or mechanisms used to make a rule, ruling, or obligation effective.

### Compliance

Following a rule, judgment, award, treaty, or obligation.

### Enforcement Gap

The gap between a rule or ruling and actual behavior.

### Legal Victory

Success in a legal process, such as winning a judgment or arbitral award.

### Practical Enforcement

Real-world implementation of a legal result.

### Binding Decision

A legal decision that parties are obligated to follow.

### Arbitral Award

A decision issued by an arbitral tribunal.

### UNCLOS Article 296

The UNCLOS provision stating that certain decisions by a court or tribunal with jurisdiction are final and shall be complied with by the parties to the dispute.

### Annex VII Arbitration

A dispute settlement procedure under UNCLOS using an arbitral tribunal.

### State Sovereignty

The authority of a state to govern itself and control its territory and political decisions.

### Non-Compliance

Failure or refusal to follow a legal obligation or decision.

### Reputational Pressure

Pressure created by concern for how a state is viewed by others.

### Diplomatic Pressure

Pressure applied through statements, protests, negotiations, alliances, or international forums.

### Sanctions

Penalties such as trade restrictions, asset freezes, arms embargoes, or travel bans.

### Deterrence

Discouraging another actor from taking unwanted action by increasing the expected costs or risks.

### Gray-Zone Activity

Coercive activity below the threshold of open armed conflict.

### Maritime Presence

The use of coast guard, naval, civilian, or surveillance presence to assert rights or monitor activity at sea.

### Sovereign Rights

Specific rights a coastal state has in maritime zones such as the EEZ, especially over resources.

### Nine-Dash Line

A line on Chinese maps associated with China’s claims in the South China Sea.

### Rules-Based Order

An international system in which states are expected to follow agreed rules and legal principles.

### Security Council Veto

The power of a permanent UN Security Council member to block a substantive decision.

### ASEAN Consensus

ASEAN’s practice of making decisions through broad agreement among member states.

### International Legitimacy

The perception that a position, rule, or institution is lawful, fair, and acceptable.
