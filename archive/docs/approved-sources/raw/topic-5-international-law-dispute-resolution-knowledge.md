# Topic 5: International Law and Dispute Resolution

## Short Overview

International law is the body of rules, principles, procedures, and institutions that helps organize relations among states and other international actors. It is one of the foundations of global governance because it gives countries a shared legal language for cooperation, responsibility, and dispute settlement.

In domestic society, law is usually enforced by national institutions such as courts, police, and government agencies. International law works differently. There is no single world government and no single global police force with automatic authority over all states. Instead, international law depends on treaties, customary rules, legal institutions, diplomacy, state consent, reputation, reciprocity, political pressure, and sometimes collective action through international organizations.

International law matters because states often disagree over territory, maritime zones, trade, human rights, environmental obligations, the use of force, borders, and responsibility for harmful conduct. Without legal rules and peaceful dispute settlement, disagreements can become more dangerous and may escalate into diplomatic crises, economic conflict, or armed confrontation.

Dispute resolution is the process of managing and settling disagreements. In international affairs, disputes can be handled through negotiation, mediation, inquiry, conciliation, arbitration, judicial settlement, regional arrangements, or United Nations processes. Some methods are diplomatic and flexible. Others are legal and more formal. Together, these methods give states alternatives to force.

International law is important, but it is also limited. A state may win a legal case but still face difficulty achieving practical enforcement. A court or tribunal may clarify rights and obligations, but implementation may depend on politics, diplomacy, pressure, and state behavior. This is why the difference between **legal victory** and **practical enforcement** is one of the most important ideas in studying global governance.

## What Is International Law?

International law is a system of rules and principles that regulates relations between states and, in some areas, international organizations, individuals, corporations, and other actors. Traditionally, international law focused mainly on states because states were treated as the main legal actors in the international system. Over time, international law has expanded to cover more issues and more actors.

International law can regulate:

- treaties between states
- diplomatic relations
- use of force
- maritime rights and ocean governance
- human rights
- humanitarian law during armed conflict
- international trade
- environmental protection
- international criminal responsibility
- refugee protection
- state responsibility for wrongful acts
- peaceful settlement of disputes

A simple way to explain international law is: **international law is the set of legal rules that helps countries know what they may do, what they must do, and how they should settle disagreements with one another.**

### International Law vs Domestic Law

Domestic law is law within a country. It is made and enforced by national institutions such as legislatures, courts, police, and administrative agencies. For example, a national law may regulate taxes, schools, crimes, roads, businesses, or elections inside a state.

International law operates between states and across borders. It does not usually have the same centralized enforcement system as domestic law. There is no single world legislature, world police force, or global executive government. International law instead relies on state consent, treaties, customs, courts, international organizations, diplomatic pressure, and the willingness of states to comply.

This difference does not mean international law is fake or meaningless. It means international law functions differently from domestic law. It is weaker in some enforcement situations, but it can still shape state behavior, provide legitimacy, clarify obligations, create procedures, and support peaceful dispute settlement.

### Why International Law Still Matters

International law matters because states need predictable rules. Without shared rules, states would have fewer peaceful ways to manage disagreement. International law helps create:

- order in international relations
- predictability for states and institutions
- peaceful alternatives to force
- legal standards for cooperation
- procedures for resolving disputes
- rights and responsibilities
- legitimacy for diplomatic claims
- protection for weaker states
- shared expectations about acceptable behavior

International law does not eliminate power politics, but it gives states a framework for arguing, negotiating, and resolving disputes through law instead of only through force.

## Why International Law Matters in Global Governance

Global governance depends on international law because many global problems require shared rules. Global governance is not a world government. It is a system of coordination among states, institutions, and other actors. International law gives that coordination a legal structure.

### Peace and Security

International law helps regulate the use of force, peaceful settlement of disputes, sanctions, self-defense, peacekeeping, and collective security. The United Nations Charter is central to this area because it requires states to settle disputes peacefully and limits the threat or use of force.

### Human Rights

International human rights law sets standards for the treatment of individuals and groups. Human rights treaties, declarations, monitoring bodies, and courts help define rights and responsibilities. Enforcement varies, but human rights law provides a global language for accountability and protection.

### Trade

International economic law supports trade rules, market access, dispute settlement, and predictability in cross-border commerce. Trade law helps states manage disagreements over tariffs, subsidies, goods, services, and trade barriers.

### Environmental Protection

Environmental problems often cross borders. International environmental law helps states cooperate on climate change, biodiversity, pollution, oceans, and conservation. Environmental agreements can set goals, reporting duties, principles, and cooperation mechanisms.

### Maritime Order

The law of the sea is a major part of international law. It helps define maritime zones, navigation rights, resource rights, and dispute settlement procedures. The United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea, or UNCLOS, is especially important for ocean governance.

### Diplomacy

Diplomatic law supports communication among states by protecting diplomats, embassies, and official relations. It helps states communicate even during serious disagreement.

### Humanitarian Protection

International humanitarian law regulates armed conflict. It aims to protect civilians, prisoners, wounded persons, and others affected by war. It also regulates methods and means of warfare.

### Territorial and Maritime Disputes

International law provides rules and procedures for disputes over land, islands, seas, boundaries, and maritime entitlements. Courts and tribunals can clarify legal rights, but political acceptance and enforcement may still be difficult.

### Global Cooperation

International law supports cooperation on public health, migration, technology, aviation, shipping, food security, development, and many other issues. It gives states a way to make commitments and coordinate action.

## Sources of International Law

The main sources of international law are commonly described through Article 38 of the Statute of the International Court of Justice. Article 38 identifies the materials that the Court applies when deciding disputes submitted to it.

The major sources include:

- international conventions or treaties
- international custom
- general principles of law
- judicial decisions
- teachings of highly qualified publicists, or scholarly writings, as subsidiary means

These sources do not all operate in exactly the same way. Treaties and custom are often treated as primary sources. Judicial decisions and scholarly writings are usually treated as subsidiary means for determining legal rules, meaning they help identify or interpret law.

## Treaties and Conventions

A treaty is a written agreement between states or other subjects of international law that is governed by international law. Treaties may also be called conventions, covenants, protocols, agreements, or charters. The name can vary, but the key idea is that states consent to legal obligations.

Examples of treaties include:

- the UN Charter
- the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea
- human rights treaties
- climate agreements
- trade agreements
- arms control treaties
- peace agreements
- aviation and shipping conventions

### Signature

Signature often shows that a state supports a treaty or intends to consider becoming bound by it. In many cases, signature alone does not complete the process of becoming legally bound. The exact legal effect depends on the treaty and the state’s legal system.

### Ratification

Ratification is a formal act by which a state confirms its consent to be bound by a treaty. Many treaties require ratification after signature. Ratification often follows domestic legal procedures, such as legislative approval.

### Accession

Accession is a way for a state to join a treaty that it did not originally sign. A state may accede to a treaty after it has entered into force.

### Reservations

A reservation is a statement by a state that seeks to exclude or modify the legal effect of certain treaty provisions as they apply to that state. Not all reservations are allowed. Some treaties prohibit reservations, and reservations cannot defeat the object and purpose of a treaty.

### Treaty Obligations

When a state becomes party to a treaty, it accepts legal obligations under that treaty. A basic principle of treaty law is that agreements must be kept. This principle is often described by the Latin phrase **pacta sunt servanda**.

Treaties matter because they allow states to create clear legal commitments. However, treaties also depend on consent and implementation. A treaty may set obligations, but states still need to apply them through domestic law, policy, institutions, funding, and cooperation.

## Customary International Law

Customary international law is law that develops from the general practice of states accepted as law. It is not always written in one treaty. Instead, it emerges from what states generally do and what they believe they are legally required or permitted to do.

Customary international law has two main elements:

1. **State practice** — the actual behavior of states.
2. **Opinio juris** — the belief that the practice is carried out because of a legal obligation or legal right.

A simple explanation is: **customary international law forms when states generally behave in a certain way and treat that behavior as legally required or legally allowed.**

### Examples

Examples often associated with customary international law include:

- certain rules of diplomatic immunity
- prohibitions against genocide
- some basic principles of humanitarian law
- rules related to state responsibility
- principles concerning the use of force and non-intervention, depending on context

Customary international law matters because not every legal rule is found in a treaty that all states have joined. Custom can apply broadly and can influence treaty interpretation, court decisions, and state behavior.

### Challenges

Customary international law can be difficult to prove. Lawyers and courts may need to examine state practice, official statements, votes, diplomatic correspondence, military manuals, national laws, court decisions, and other evidence. States may disagree over whether a custom exists or what it requires.

## General Principles of Law

General principles of law are principles recognized across legal systems that can help fill gaps or guide legal reasoning. They are listed in Article 38 of the ICJ Statute as one of the sources the Court may apply.

Examples may include principles such as good faith, responsibility for wrongful acts, fairness in procedure, and other broad legal ideas recognized in many legal systems.

General principles are important because international law may not always have a detailed treaty or custom for every situation. These principles can help courts reason through legal disputes and maintain coherence in the legal system.

## Judicial Decisions and Scholarly Writings

Judicial decisions and scholarly writings are considered subsidiary means for determining rules of law. This means they help identify, explain, and interpret international law.

### Judicial Decisions

International court and tribunal decisions can clarify legal rules. For example, decisions of the International Court of Justice, arbitral tribunals, and other courts may influence future legal arguments. However, court decisions are usually binding only on the parties in the specific case and do not automatically create law for all states in the same way a treaty might.

### Scholarly Writings

The writings of respected scholars can help explain legal doctrines, interpret sources, and organize legal arguments. Scholars do not create binding law simply by writing, but their work can influence courts, governments, and legal debates.

## State Sovereignty and International Law

Sovereignty means that a state has legal authority over its territory, population, and political independence. International law is built around sovereign states, but it also limits and organizes how states behave.

There is a tension between sovereignty and international obligation. On one hand, states are independent and cannot usually be ruled by an outside authority without consent. On the other hand, states can accept legal obligations through treaties, customary international law, and membership in international organizations.

### How Sovereign States Accept Legal Obligations

States may accept obligations by:

- joining treaties
- participating in international organizations
- recognizing court jurisdiction
- following customary law
- making declarations
- entering arbitration agreements
- adopting domestic laws to implement international commitments

### Why States Comply with International Law

States may comply with international law for several reasons:

- they believe they are legally obligated
- they want to maintain reputation
- they expect reciprocity from other states
- they want stable diplomatic relations
- they face pressure from allies or institutions
- they want economic benefits
- they want to avoid sanctions or retaliation
- domestic courts or public opinion support compliance
- compliance supports long-term national interest

### Why States Sometimes Resist International Law

States may resist or ignore international law when:

- they believe the law conflicts with national interest
- they reject a court or tribunal’s jurisdiction
- they think enforcement will be weak
- domestic politics reward non-compliance
- powerful actors protect them
- they disagree with the interpretation of the law
- they prioritize strategic or military goals
- they believe other states are acting unfairly

This tension is central to global governance. International law depends on state consent and cooperation, but it also seeks to shape and restrain state behavior.

## Peaceful Settlement of Disputes

The peaceful settlement of disputes is a core principle of the UN Charter and international law. Article 2 of the UN Charter requires members to settle international disputes by peaceful means in a way that does not endanger international peace, security, and justice.

Chapter VI of the UN Charter identifies peaceful methods that states should seek when a dispute may endanger international peace and security. Article 33 lists negotiation, enquiry, mediation, conciliation, arbitration, judicial settlement, resort to regional agencies or arrangements, and other peaceful means of the parties’ choice.

Peaceful dispute settlement is important because it gives states alternatives to force. Disputes can involve borders, maritime claims, trade, environmental harm, treaty interpretation, use of force, diplomatic protection, or responsibility for wrongful acts. If states have peaceful procedures, they are less likely to escalate immediately to coercion or armed conflict.

### Diplomatic Methods and Legal Methods

Peaceful dispute settlement methods can be grouped into two broad categories.

**Diplomatic methods** include negotiation, mediation, good offices, inquiry, and conciliation. They are often flexible and political. They may help parties communicate, clarify facts, or compromise.

**Legal methods** include arbitration and judicial settlement. They usually involve applying legal rules to the dispute and producing a decision, award, judgment, or opinion.

These categories can overlap. For example, arbitration is legal but can be designed flexibly. Conciliation may involve fact-finding and legal reasoning but usually produces recommendations rather than binding judgments.

## Negotiation

Negotiation is the most direct method of dispute settlement. It happens when states or parties communicate directly to try to resolve a disagreement.

Negotiation can be formal or informal. It can occur through diplomats, ministers, heads of government, technical experts, or special envoys. It may be private or public. It may involve written proposals, meetings, summits, or back-channel communication.

### Strengths of Negotiation

Negotiation has several strengths:

- It is flexible.
- It allows direct communication.
- It can protect confidentiality.
- It can preserve relationships.
- It allows parties to create practical compromises.
- It does not require a third party.
- It can address political, legal, economic, and security issues together.

### Limitations of Negotiation

Negotiation also has limits:

- A stronger state may pressure a weaker state.
- Talks can reach deadlock.
- Parties may not trust each other.
- There is no guaranteed outcome.
- Negotiations can be used to delay action.
- Domestic politics may prevent compromise.
- The agreement may be vague or poorly implemented.

Negotiation is often the first step in dispute settlement because it is direct and flexible. However, if negotiation fails, parties may turn to mediation, arbitration, courts, or international organizations.

## Mediation and Good Offices

Mediation involves a third party helping disputing parties communicate, understand each other’s positions, and consider possible solutions. The mediator does not usually impose a binding decision. Instead, the mediator assists the parties in reaching agreement.

Good offices are similar but more limited. A third party providing good offices may help bring parties together, create communication channels, or arrange talks without actively proposing detailed solutions.

### Possible Mediators

Mediators or providers of good offices may include:

- the UN Secretary-General
- UN envoys or representatives
- regional organizations
- neutral states
- respected diplomats
- religious leaders
- former officials
- international organizations
- groups of states

### Strengths

Mediation and good offices can:

- reduce tension
- improve communication
- allow face-saving compromise
- help parties clarify interests
- provide neutral facilitation
- support ceasefires or peace talks
- combine law, politics, and practical solutions

### Limitations

They also face limits:

- parties must be willing to participate
- mediators may be seen as biased
- recommendations are usually not binding
- powerful actors may ignore the process
- talks can collapse
- agreements may not be implemented

Mediation is useful when the dispute is political, sensitive, or complex and when parties need help communicating but are not ready to accept a court decision.

## Inquiry and Fact-Finding

Inquiry and fact-finding focus on establishing facts. Many international disputes involve disagreement not only about law but also about what actually happened. For example, states may disagree about who crossed a border, whether an attack occurred, what caused environmental harm, or whether a treaty was violated.

Fact-finding can be carried out by:

- commissions of inquiry
- expert panels
- UN missions
- technical bodies
- human rights investigators
- independent experts
- regional organizations
- treaty monitoring bodies

### Why Fact-Finding Matters

Fact-finding matters because legal and political solutions often require a shared understanding of evidence. If parties disagree about basic facts, negotiation or legal settlement becomes harder.

### Limitations

Fact-finding can be limited by:

- lack of access to territory
- refusal to cooperate
- security risks
- biased evidence
- missing documents
- witness intimidation
- disagreement over methods
- politicization of findings

Fact-finding does not automatically solve a dispute, but it can reduce uncertainty and support later negotiation, mediation, arbitration, or judicial settlement.

## Conciliation

Conciliation is a method of dispute settlement that combines investigation, discussion, and proposed settlement. A conciliator or commission may examine the dispute, hear from the parties, assess facts and legal issues, and propose terms for settlement.

Conciliation is often more structured than mediation but usually less binding than arbitration or judicial settlement. It can be useful when states want outside help but do not want a court judgment.

### Strengths

Conciliation can:

- clarify facts and law
- provide neutral recommendations
- reduce tension
- help parties compromise
- avoid the adversarial character of litigation
- allow flexible solutions

### Limitations

Conciliation has limits:

- recommendations may not be binding
- parties may reject the proposed settlement
- success depends on cooperation
- powerful parties may resist compromise
- it may not work where legal positions are deeply entrenched

Conciliation is useful when parties want a structured process but still want control over whether to accept the outcome.

## Arbitration

Arbitration is a legal method of dispute settlement in which parties agree to submit a dispute to one or more arbitrators. The arbitrators examine the dispute and issue an arbitral award. If the parties have agreed that the award is binding, they are legally expected to comply.

Arbitration is different from a permanent court. A court such as the ICJ has a standing institution and judges. Arbitration can be created for a specific dispute, with arbitrators selected by the parties or appointed according to agreed rules.

### How Arbitration Works

In international arbitration, parties may agree on:

- the issues to be decided
- the applicable law
- the number of arbitrators
- the procedure
- the language
- the timetable
- the institution administering the case
- confidentiality or publication rules
- the effect of the award

Arbitration can be used in disputes between states, disputes involving international organizations, investor-state disputes, and some disputes involving private entities.

### Arbitral Awards

An arbitral award is the decision of an arbitral tribunal. If the arbitration agreement provides that the award is binding, the parties are expected to comply. However, practical enforcement can still be difficult, especially when the losing party is a state that rejects the award or refuses to cooperate.

### Strengths

Arbitration has several strengths:

- flexible procedure
- legal reasoning
- party participation in selecting arbitrators
- usefulness for technical disputes
- potential confidentiality
- binding awards when agreed
- adaptability to different kinds of disputes

### Limitations

Arbitration also has limits:

- jurisdiction depends on consent or a legal basis
- proceedings can be expensive
- awards may face resistance
- enforcement may be politically difficult
- parties may dispute the tribunal’s authority
- powerful states may reject outcomes

Arbitration is an important tool because it combines law with procedural flexibility.

## Judicial Settlement

Judicial settlement means submitting a dispute to an international court or tribunal. Courts apply legal rules and issue judgments, orders, or advisory opinions depending on their jurisdiction.

Judicial settlement is more formal than negotiation or mediation. It is useful when parties need an authoritative interpretation of law. Courts can clarify rights, duties, jurisdiction, boundaries, or treaty meanings.

### Strengths

Judicial settlement can:

- clarify legal obligations
- create an official legal record
- reduce reliance on force
- support peaceful dispute resolution
- provide reasoned decisions
- strengthen the rule of law
- help smaller states use legal arguments against stronger states

### Limitations

Judicial settlement has limits:

- courts need jurisdiction
- state consent is often required
- proceedings can take years
- litigation can be expensive
- legal victory may not produce immediate enforcement
- some disputes are too politically sensitive for court settlement
- parties may reject or minimize decisions

Judicial settlement is important, but it is not a magic solution. It is one part of a wider system of law, diplomacy, and politics.

## The International Court of Justice

The International Court of Justice, or ICJ, is the principal judicial organ of the United Nations. It is located in The Hague, Netherlands. It was established by the UN Charter and operates under the Statute of the International Court of Justice.

The ICJ has two main roles:

1. It settles legal disputes between states in contentious cases.
2. It gives advisory opinions on legal questions referred to it by authorized UN organs and specialized agencies.

### Contentious Cases

Contentious cases are disputes between states. Only states may be parties in contentious cases before the ICJ. Individuals, corporations, NGOs, and private groups cannot bring contentious cases as parties before the ICJ.

The Court’s jurisdiction depends on state consent. This consent can come from:

- a special agreement between states
- a treaty clause giving the ICJ jurisdiction
- optional declarations accepting compulsory jurisdiction
- other recognized legal bases

This means the ICJ cannot automatically decide every dispute between states. It must have jurisdiction.

### Advisory Opinions

Advisory opinions are legal opinions requested by authorized UN organs or specialized agencies. They are not the same as judgments in contentious cases. Advisory opinions are generally not binding in the same way as judgments between parties, but they can carry major legal, political, and moral weight.

Advisory opinions can clarify international law and influence state behavior, UN processes, domestic courts, and public debate.

### Judgments

ICJ judgments in contentious cases are binding between the parties to the case. If a party fails to comply with a judgment, the other party may raise the matter with the Security Council under the UN Charter. However, practical enforcement can still be difficult because Security Council action is political and may be affected by veto power and state interests.

### Difference Between the ICJ and the ICC

The ICJ is not the same as the International Criminal Court.

The ICJ deals mainly with legal disputes between states and advisory opinions. It is the principal judicial organ of the UN.

The ICC deals with individual criminal responsibility for crimes such as genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes, and aggression under its own legal framework. The ICC is not one of the six principal organs of the UN.

### Strengths of the ICJ

The ICJ is important because it:

- provides legal settlement between states
- clarifies international law
- supports peaceful dispute resolution
- gives advisory opinions on major legal questions
- gives smaller states a legal forum
- contributes to the rule of law in international affairs

### Limitations of the ICJ

The ICJ has limitations:

- only states can be parties in contentious cases
- jurisdiction often depends on state consent
- cases can take time
- enforcement can be difficult
- political resistance can limit practical impact
- not all disputes are submitted to the Court

A simple explanation is: **the ICJ is the UN’s main court for legal disputes between states, but it cannot hear every dispute unless it has jurisdiction, and it cannot enforce every judgment by itself.**

## The Permanent Court of Arbitration

The Permanent Court of Arbitration, or PCA, is an intergovernmental organization that provides dispute resolution services. It was established by treaty in 1899. Despite its name, it is not a permanent court in the same way as the ICJ. It does not have one fixed bench of judges deciding every case. Instead, it helps administer arbitration and other dispute resolution proceedings.

The PCA can support disputes involving:

- states
- state entities
- international organizations
- private parties in certain proceedings
- mixed disputes depending on the legal basis

### What the PCA Does

The PCA may provide:

- administrative support
- registry services
- hearing facilities
- appointment authority services
- procedural support
- support for arbitral tribunals
- publication of materials where appropriate

The PCA matters because arbitration is a major method of peaceful dispute resolution. It allows parties to create specialized tribunals for specific disputes.

### Strengths

The PCA has strengths:

- flexibility
- experience in state-related arbitration
- ability to administer technical and complex disputes
- support for different types of parties
- adaptability to different procedural rules
- usefulness in maritime, boundary, investment, environmental, and treaty disputes

### Limitations

The PCA also has limits:

- it does not automatically have jurisdiction over every dispute
- arbitration depends on consent or a legal basis
- the PCA itself does not enforce awards like a world police force
- parties may challenge or resist outcomes
- enforcement depends on legal, diplomatic, and political processes

A simple explanation is: **the PCA helps administer arbitration between states and other international actors. It supports legal dispute settlement, but it is not the same as a standing world court.**

## International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea

The International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea, or ITLOS, is an international tribunal created under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea. It is located in Hamburg, Germany. It handles disputes related to the interpretation or application of UNCLOS and other agreements that give it jurisdiction.

ITLOS is important because many disputes involve oceans, maritime zones, navigation, fisheries, seabed resources, marine environment, and maritime boundaries.

### Jurisdiction

ITLOS jurisdiction concerns disputes submitted to it under UNCLOS and other agreements that confer jurisdiction. UNCLOS provides a dispute settlement system under Part XV. States may choose among different procedures, including ITLOS, the ICJ, Annex VII arbitration, or special arbitration under Annex VIII.

### Types of Matters

ITLOS may deal with:

- prompt release of vessels and crews
- provisional measures
- maritime boundary issues where jurisdiction exists
- interpretation of UNCLOS
- protection of the marine environment
- fisheries and navigation-related disputes
- seabed-related matters through its Seabed Disputes Chamber

### Strengths

ITLOS has strengths:

- specialized knowledge of law of the sea
- ability to handle urgent provisional measures
- procedures for prompt release cases
- contribution to maritime legal clarity
- institutional focus on UNCLOS

### Limitations

ITLOS also has limits:

- jurisdiction depends on UNCLOS rules and party choices
- some disputes may be excluded or limited
- enforcement still depends on state compliance
- maritime disputes often involve sovereignty and security concerns beyond legal interpretation

A simple explanation is: **ITLOS is a specialized tribunal for disputes about the law of the sea. It helps interpret UNCLOS, but it still depends on jurisdiction and state compliance.**

## UNCLOS and Dispute Settlement

The United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea, or UNCLOS, is a major treaty governing ocean spaces and maritime rights. It is often called the “constitution for the oceans” because it provides a broad legal framework for seas, oceans, navigation, maritime zones, resources, marine scientific research, and dispute settlement.

UNCLOS is important for global governance because oceans connect states through trade, security, fisheries, energy, environment, and transportation. Maritime disputes can affect regional stability and international commerce.

## Key UNCLOS Concepts

### Territorial Sea

The territorial sea is a maritime zone extending up to 12 nautical miles from a coastal state’s baseline. In this zone, the coastal state has sovereignty, subject to certain rights such as innocent passage for foreign ships.

### Exclusive Economic Zone

The exclusive economic zone, or EEZ, extends up to 200 nautical miles from the baseline. In the EEZ, the coastal state has sovereign rights for exploring, exploiting, conserving, and managing natural resources. Other states still enjoy certain freedoms, such as navigation and overflight, consistent with UNCLOS.

### Continental Shelf

The continental shelf refers to the seabed and subsoil of submarine areas extending beyond the territorial sea, under conditions defined by UNCLOS. Coastal states have rights over resources of the continental shelf.

### High Seas

The high seas are areas beyond national jurisdiction where freedoms such as navigation, overflight, fishing, scientific research, and laying submarine cables apply under international law.

### Freedom of Navigation

Freedom of navigation is a principle allowing ships to move through ocean spaces according to international law. It is important for trade, security, and global connectivity.

### Maritime Entitlements

Maritime entitlements are the zones or rights that a coastal feature or coastal state may generate under law. Disputes may arise over whether a feature is an island, rock, low-tide elevation, or submerged feature, and what maritime zones it can generate.

## UNCLOS Dispute Settlement

Part XV of UNCLOS requires states parties to settle disputes concerning the interpretation or application of the Convention by peaceful means. If the parties cannot resolve the dispute by negotiation or other chosen peaceful means, UNCLOS provides procedures that may lead to binding decisions, subject to limitations and exceptions.

UNCLOS dispute settlement options include:

- ITLOS
- International Court of Justice
- arbitral tribunal under Annex VII
- special arbitral tribunal under Annex VIII

### Annex VII Arbitration

Annex VII arbitration is a default procedure under UNCLOS in many situations when states have not chosen the same forum. It creates an arbitral tribunal to hear a specific dispute. Annex VII arbitration has been important in maritime disputes, including disputes where one party participates and the other rejects or refuses to participate, depending on the legal circumstances.

### Annex VIII Special Arbitration

Annex VIII provides special arbitration for certain categories of disputes, including fisheries, protection and preservation of the marine environment, marine scientific research, and navigation, depending on the Convention’s terms.

### Limits and Exceptions

UNCLOS dispute settlement has limits. Some disputes may be excluded, especially disputes involving maritime boundary delimitation, historic bays or titles, military activities, or certain law enforcement activities, depending on declarations and Convention provisions. States may make declarations under Article 298 excluding some categories from compulsory procedures.

This means not every maritime dispute can automatically be fully resolved by a tribunal under UNCLOS.

## International Courts and Tribunals Beyond the ICJ

The international legal system includes multiple courts and tribunals. Students should not assume all international courts do the same thing. Each institution has its own jurisdiction, parties, procedures, and legal basis.

### International Criminal Court

The International Criminal Court focuses on individual criminal responsibility for genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes, and aggression. It is different from the ICJ, which handles disputes between states.

### Regional Human Rights Courts

Some regions have courts that hear human rights cases under regional treaties. Examples include the European Court of Human Rights, the Inter-American Court of Human Rights, and the African Court on Human and Peoples’ Rights. Their jurisdiction depends on their respective treaties and regional systems.

### Trade Dispute Settlement Bodies

Trade disputes may be handled through trade agreements and institutions such as the World Trade Organization dispute settlement system. These disputes often concern tariffs, subsidies, discrimination, market access, or trade restrictions.

### Investment Arbitration Tribunals

Investment arbitration may involve disputes between foreign investors and host states under investment treaties or contracts. These cases can involve expropriation, fair treatment, discrimination, and compensation.

### Ad Hoc and Special Tribunals

Some tribunals are created for specific conflicts or categories of crimes. These may include international criminal tribunals, hybrid tribunals, claims commissions, or other special mechanisms.

The key point is that international adjudication is fragmented. There is no single world court for every issue. Different courts serve different purposes.

## Binding Judgments, Awards, and Advisory Opinions

Legal form matters. Different legal outputs have different effects.

### Binding Judgment

A binding judgment is a court decision that legally binds the parties to the case. For example, an ICJ judgment in a contentious case is binding between the parties.

### Arbitral Award

An arbitral award is the decision of an arbitral tribunal. If the arbitration is binding, the parties are legally expected to comply with the award.

### Advisory Opinion

An advisory opinion is a legal opinion given by a court, such as the ICJ, in response to a request from an authorized body. It is not the same as a binding judgment between parties, but it can strongly influence legal interpretation, politics, and public debate.

### Recommendation

A recommendation suggests or encourages action but usually does not legally compel it.

### Political Declaration

A political declaration expresses shared commitments or principles. It can be important politically but may not create binding legal obligations unless connected to a legal instrument.

### Why Legal Form Matters

Students should not treat every legal or international statement as equally binding. A treaty, Security Council decision, court judgment, arbitral award, advisory opinion, and declaration may all matter, but they do not all have the same legal status.

## Enforcement and Compliance

Enforcement is one of the hardest parts of international law. There is no single global police authority that automatically forces every state to follow every rule, judgment, or award.

### Compliance

Compliance means following legal obligations. Many states comply with international law much of the time because it supports predictability, reputation, reciprocity, and cooperation.

### Enforcement

Enforcement means taking action to make a rule or decision effective. In international law, enforcement can be legal, political, diplomatic, economic, or reputational.

### Ways Compliance May Be Encouraged

Compliance may be encouraged through:

- reputation and legitimacy
- reciprocity
- diplomatic pressure
- sanctions
- domestic courts
- international institutions
- treaty monitoring bodies
- economic consequences
- political costs
- public pressure
- alliances and partnerships
- fear of retaliation
- desire to maintain stable relations

### Why Powerful States May Be Harder to Pressure

Powerful states may be harder to pressure because they may have more economic, military, diplomatic, or institutional influence. They may also have allies willing to protect them. In some cases, veto power in the UN Security Council can prevent collective enforcement action.

However, powerful states still often care about international law. They may use legal arguments to justify their actions, protect reputation, influence allies, maintain order, or shape rules in their favor.

### Weak Enforcement Does Not Mean Law Is Meaningless

Weak enforcement does not mean international law has no effect. International law can:

- clarify rights and obligations
- shape diplomatic pressure
- support coalitions
- influence domestic courts
- affect reputations
- provide language for accountability
- guide negotiations
- create records of responsibility
- support long-term norm development

International law may be imperfect, but it remains an important part of global governance.

## The Enforcement Gap

An enforcement gap happens when a legal rule, judgment, or award exists but implementation is weak, delayed, resisted, or incomplete. This is a central concept in global governance.

### Why Enforcement Gaps Happen

Enforcement gaps can happen because of:

- lack of central global authority
- state sovereignty
- power politics
- Security Council veto power
- weak institutions
- lack of political will
- unclear jurisdiction
- domestic opposition
- limited resources
- strategic interests
- disagreement over interpretation
- fear of escalation
- dependence on state cooperation

### Why the Enforcement Gap Matters

The enforcement gap matters because global governance often creates rules without always creating strong enforcement. A legal decision may clarify what the law requires, but implementation may still depend on states and politics.

This is especially important in maritime and territorial disputes. A tribunal may clarify legal entitlements, but the losing state may refuse to comply, or physical control may remain unchanged. This does not erase the legal significance of the ruling, but it shows the limits of law without political enforcement.

## Legal Victory vs Practical Enforcement

A legal victory means a state or party succeeds in a legal proceeding. A court or tribunal may confirm its rights, reject another party’s claim, or clarify the law in its favor.

Practical enforcement means turning that legal result into actual behavior on the ground or at sea. This can be harder.

A state may win a legal case but still face problems such as:

- the losing state refuses to comply
- there is no direct enforcement mechanism
- the dispute involves powerful states
- the issue is tied to military or strategic interests
- the decision requires long-term diplomacy
- domestic politics prevent compromise
- physical control remains disputed
- regional actors avoid confrontation
- international pressure is weak

Legal victory can still matter. It can strengthen legitimacy, clarify rights, support diplomatic pressure, influence allies, shape international opinion, and create a legal record. But legal victory does not automatically end a dispute.

This distinction is important for understanding disputes where international law says one thing but political reality makes enforcement difficult.

## International Law and Maritime Disputes

Maritime disputes often involve international law because oceans are governed by legal rules about maritime zones, navigation, resources, and rights. UNCLOS is the central treaty framework for many maritime issues.

Maritime disputes may involve:

- territorial seas
- exclusive economic zones
- continental shelves
- islands
- rocks
- reefs
- low-tide elevations
- fishing rights
- oil and gas resources
- freedom of navigation
- military activities
- marine environment
- maritime boundaries
- historic claims

### Why Maritime Disputes Are Difficult

Maritime disputes can be difficult because law, geography, resources, sovereignty, nationalism, and military power often overlap.

A dispute may involve legal questions, such as whether a feature generates an EEZ. It may also involve political questions, such as national identity or strategic control. It may involve economic questions, such as fisheries or energy resources. It may involve security concerns, such as naval access or military presence.

UNCLOS provides rules and procedures, but it cannot automatically remove all political conflict.

### UNCLOS and Maritime Order

UNCLOS helps maritime governance by defining zones and rights. It provides rules for navigation, resource use, marine environmental protection, and dispute settlement. It gives states a shared legal framework for maritime claims.

This framework is important for disputes in areas such as the South China Sea / West Philippine Sea, where legal claims, navigation, resources, and power politics intersect.

## International Law and Territorial Disputes

Territorial disputes involve disagreement over sovereignty over land, islands, borders, or territory. These disputes are often sensitive because territory is connected to identity, security, resources, history, and political legitimacy.

International law can be relevant through:

- treaties
- boundary agreements
- historical documents
- maps
- effective control
- occupation
- self-determination
- uti possidetis principles in some contexts
- recognition
- peaceful settlement procedures

Territorial disputes are difficult because legal arguments may compete with historical narratives, nationalism, strategic interests, and physical control.

International law can help clarify claims, but it may not always produce immediate political settlement. Even when a legal decision exists, implementation may require negotiation, domestic acceptance, and diplomatic pressure.

## International Law, Power, and Politics

International law does not operate separately from power politics. States use law, interpret law, resist law, and sometimes shape law according to their interests. This does not make law meaningless. It means law and politics are deeply connected.

### Powerful States

Powerful states can influence the development and enforcement of international law. They may shape treaty negotiations, use veto power, influence institutions, or resist legal outcomes. They may also use international law to justify their actions or criticize others.

### Smaller States

Smaller states can also use international law. Legal rules can help weaker states build legitimacy, attract support, form coalitions, challenge stronger states, and frame disputes in legal terms rather than pure power terms.

### Law as a Tool of Order and Contestation

International law can support order by creating rules. It can also become a field of contestation, where states argue about interpretation, legitimacy, jurisdiction, and compliance.

This is why international law should not be understood as either all-powerful or useless. It is a tool that interacts with diplomacy, institutions, political pressure, and power.

## Strengths of International Law and Dispute Resolution

International law and dispute resolution have several strengths.

### Peaceful Alternatives to Force

They give states ways to manage disputes without immediately using force.

### Clearer Rules

They clarify what states may claim, what they must do, and how they should behave.

### Legal Predictability

They help states plan behavior and understand consequences.

### Legitimacy

Legal arguments can strengthen a state’s diplomatic position and public credibility.

### Diplomatic Pressure

Legal decisions and obligations can support pressure from other states, organizations, and publics.

### Protection of Weaker States

International law can help smaller or weaker states challenge stronger states through legal claims and international institutions.

### Development of Norms

Legal decisions, treaties, and practice can develop norms over time.

### Evidence and Reasoning

Legal processes create records, evidence, and reasoned decisions that can shape future debate.

### Support for Global Governance

International law supports cooperation in trade, environment, health, human rights, maritime order, and security.

## Limits and Criticisms

International law and dispute resolution also face major limitations.

### Weak Enforcement

There is no single global police authority. Implementation often depends on states.

### Dependence on State Consent

Many courts and tribunals need state consent or treaty-based jurisdiction.

### Unequal Power

Powerful states may resist legal decisions or shape enforcement.

### Slow Proceedings

Legal cases can take years.

### High Legal Costs

International litigation and arbitration can be expensive.

### Selective Compliance

States may comply when it benefits them and resist when it does not.

### Political Resistance

Domestic politics, nationalism, and strategic interests can block implementation.

### Limited Jurisdiction

A court or tribunal can only decide issues within its jurisdiction.

### Lack of Centralized Authority

International law operates in a decentralized system of sovereign states.

These limitations do not mean international law is useless. They show why law, diplomacy, institutions, and politics must be studied together.

## Common Misconceptions

### “International law is fake because there is no world police.”

This is incorrect. International law does not work like domestic law, but it still shapes state behavior, diplomacy, courts, treaties, reputation, and global governance.

### “International courts can automatically force countries to obey.”

This is incorrect. Courts can issue judgments or awards where they have jurisdiction, but enforcement often depends on state compliance, political pressure, institutions, or further action.

### “All international legal decisions are binding on everyone.”

This is incorrect. Some decisions bind only the parties. Advisory opinions may not be binding in the same way. Recommendations and declarations usually have different legal effects.

### “The ICJ and ICC are the same court.”

This is incorrect. The ICJ handles disputes between states and advisory opinions. The ICC handles individual criminal responsibility.

### “Arbitration is not legal because it is not a regular court.”

This is incorrect. Arbitration is a recognized legal method of dispute settlement when based on consent or a legal instrument.

### “If a country wins a case, the dispute is automatically solved.”

This is incorrect. Legal victory can clarify rights, but practical enforcement may require diplomacy, pressure, implementation, and political acceptance.

### “Powerful states never care about international law.”

This is too simple. Powerful states may violate, resist, or shape international law, but they also often use legal arguments, treaties, institutions, and rules to protect interests and legitimacy.

### “Small states cannot use international law.”

This is incorrect. Smaller states can use international law to build legitimacy, create coalitions, and challenge stronger states through legal institutions.

### “UNCLOS only matters for lawyers.”

This is incorrect. UNCLOS affects navigation, fisheries, energy, trade, maritime security, environmental protection, and territorial disputes.

### “International law and politics are completely separate.”

This is incorrect. International law and politics interact constantly. Legal rules shape politics, and political power affects legal enforcement.

## Key Terms and Simple Explanations

### International Law

Rules and principles that regulate relations among states and other international actors.

### Treaty

A written legal agreement between states or other international legal actors.

### Convention

A type of treaty, often used for major multilateral agreements.

### Customary International Law

Law that develops from general state practice accepted as legally required or legally permitted.

### State Sovereignty

The legal authority of a state over its territory, people, and political independence.

### Jurisdiction

The legal authority of a court, tribunal, or state to decide or regulate a matter.

### Peaceful Settlement of Disputes

The principle that states should resolve disagreements through peaceful methods rather than force.

### Negotiation

Direct discussion between parties to reach agreement.

### Mediation

A third party helps disputing parties communicate and seek a solution.

### Good Offices

A third party helps parties begin or maintain communication, often without proposing detailed terms.

### Inquiry

A process for investigating facts in a dispute.

### Conciliation

A structured process that investigates a dispute and proposes a settlement, usually not binding unless accepted.

### Arbitration

A legal process where parties submit a dispute to arbitrators who issue an award.

### Judicial Settlement

Settlement of a dispute by an international court or tribunal.

### International Court of Justice

The principal judicial organ of the UN, handling disputes between states and advisory opinions.

### Permanent Court of Arbitration

An intergovernmental organization that provides arbitration and dispute resolution services.

### International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea

A tribunal created under UNCLOS to handle disputes concerning the law of the sea.

### UNCLOS

The United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea, the main treaty framework for ocean governance.

### Advisory Opinion

A legal opinion given by a court in response to a request from an authorized body.

### Judgment

A formal decision by a court in a legal case.

### Arbitral Award

A decision issued by an arbitral tribunal.

### Compliance

Following a legal obligation or decision.

### Enforcement

Actions taken to make a rule, judgment, or award effective.

### Enforcement Gap

The gap between having a legal rule or decision and actually implementing it in practice.

### State Responsibility

The legal responsibility of a state for an internationally wrongful act.

### Maritime Dispute

A dispute involving ocean spaces, maritime zones, navigation, resources, or sea boundaries.

### Territorial Dispute

A dispute over sovereignty or control of land, islands, borders, or territory.

### Exclusive Economic Zone

A maritime zone extending up to 200 nautical miles where a coastal state has sovereign rights over natural resources.

### Continental Shelf

The seabed and subsoil areas over which a coastal state has rights to resources under UNCLOS rules.
