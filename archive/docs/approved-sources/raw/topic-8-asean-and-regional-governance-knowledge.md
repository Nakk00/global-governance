# Topic 8: ASEAN and Regional Governance

## Short Overview

ASEAN is one of the most important examples of regional governance in the world. It shows how neighboring states can cooperate on security, economics, diplomacy, development, disaster response, public health, migration, environment, and regional stability while still protecting national sovereignty.

ASEAN stands for the Association of Southeast Asian Nations. It is a regional intergovernmental organization made up of sovereign states in Southeast Asia. It is not a country, not a world government, and not a supranational state. ASEAN does not operate like a single central government above its members. Instead, it works through consultation, consensus, diplomacy, regional agreements, cooperation mechanisms, and regular meetings among member states.

ASEAN matters in global governance because many problems are not only national or global; they are also regional. Southeast Asian states share borders, sea lanes, rivers, trade routes, migration flows, disaster risks, environmental challenges, and security concerns. Regional governance helps states manage problems that are too large for one country alone but often too specific for global institutions to handle directly.

ASEAN also illustrates the strengths and limits of sovereignty-respecting governance. Its principles of consultation, consensus, non-interference, peaceful settlement of disputes, and respect for sovereignty help maintain regional unity among very diverse states. However, the same principles can also make ASEAN slow, cautious, and weak in enforcement. This is especially visible in issues such as the South China Sea, Myanmar, human rights, haze pollution, and regional crisis response.

A simple student-friendly explanation is: **ASEAN is a regional organization where Southeast Asian countries cooperate while still keeping their sovereignty.**

## What Is ASEAN?

ASEAN stands for the Association of Southeast Asian Nations. It is a regional intergovernmental organization in Southeast Asia. “Intergovernmental” means that its members are governments of sovereign states. ASEAN is built on cooperation among states, not on a single authority ruling above them.

ASEAN was created to promote peace, stability, cooperation, economic development, and regional resilience in Southeast Asia. It provides a platform where member states can meet regularly, discuss common issues, build trust, negotiate agreements, coordinate policies, and manage regional challenges.

ASEAN works through:

- summits among leaders
- ministerial meetings
- sectoral bodies
- regional agreements
- consultation and consensus
- diplomatic dialogue
- ASEAN-led forums with external partners
- community-building across political-security, economic, and socio-cultural areas

ASEAN is important because Southeast Asia is a highly diverse and strategically located region. Its member states differ in size, population, wealth, political systems, religions, languages, colonial histories, and foreign policy priorities. ASEAN helps these states maintain communication and cooperation despite their differences.

ASEAN is also important because Southeast Asia sits at the center of major global trade routes and major-power competition. ASEAN gives Southeast Asian states a collective diplomatic platform so that they are not only passive objects of great-power rivalry.

## ASEAN Member States

As of the current source base, ASEAN has eleven member states. Timor-Leste was officially admitted as ASEAN’s eleventh member on 26 October 2025. Many older ASEAN documents and educational materials list ten members because Timor-Leste’s full membership came later.

The ASEAN member states are:

### Brunei Darussalam

Brunei is a small but wealthy state on the island of Borneo. It has significant energy resources and plays a role in ASEAN diplomacy, economic cooperation, and regional consensus-building.

### Cambodia

Cambodia is a mainland Southeast Asian state. It joined ASEAN in 1999 and has participated in ASEAN’s political, economic, and socio-cultural cooperation. Cambodia’s foreign policy relationships can affect ASEAN consensus on some regional issues.

### Indonesia

Indonesia is the largest ASEAN member by population and geography. It is an archipelagic state and one of the most influential countries in ASEAN. Indonesia has often supported ASEAN centrality, regional diplomacy, and the idea of Southeast Asia as a zone of peace and cooperation.

### Lao PDR

Lao PDR, commonly called Laos, is a landlocked mainland Southeast Asian state. It is important in Mekong-region cooperation, connectivity, infrastructure, and development-gap issues.

### Malaysia

Malaysia is a maritime and mainland Southeast Asian state. It is involved in ASEAN economic integration, South China Sea diplomacy, trade, and regional institution-building.

### Myanmar

Myanmar is a mainland Southeast Asian state. It is a major challenge for ASEAN because the political and humanitarian crisis after the 2021 military coup has tested ASEAN’s principles of non-interference, consensus, human rights, and regional stability.

### Philippines

The Philippines is an archipelagic state and one of the founding ASEAN members. It is central to ASEAN discussions on maritime security, the West Philippine Sea / South China Sea, migration, labor mobility, disaster response, and regional diplomacy.

### Singapore

Singapore is a small but highly developed city-state. It is a major financial, trade, logistics, and diplomatic hub. Singapore is influential in ASEAN economic integration, connectivity, and external relations.

### Thailand

Thailand is a mainland Southeast Asian state and one of the founding ASEAN members. It plays a role in mainland regional diplomacy, trade, connectivity, and political-security cooperation.

### Viet Nam

Viet Nam is a mainland and maritime Southeast Asian state. It is important in ASEAN economic growth, South China Sea issues, regional security, and supply chains.

### Timor-Leste

Timor-Leste became ASEAN’s eleventh member in 2025. Its admission marked ASEAN’s first expansion since the 1990s. Timor-Leste’s membership is important for regional inclusiveness, but it also raises practical questions about capacity-building, economic integration, and participation in ASEAN’s many meetings and mechanisms.

### Why ASEAN Diversity Matters

ASEAN members differ greatly in population, economic size, level of development, political system, religion, language, and strategic alignment. This diversity makes ASEAN cooperation valuable because it provides a common platform. It also makes cooperation difficult because members do not always have the same priorities.

For example, maritime claimant states may care more about the South China Sea, while non-claimant states may prefer caution. Wealthier states may prioritize trade and investment liberalization, while less developed states may need more time and support. Some states may emphasize sovereignty and non-interference, while others may support more active regional responses to crises.

ASEAN’s diversity explains why consensus is both useful and limiting.

## Historical Background: Why ASEAN Was Created

ASEAN was created on 8 August 1967 through the Bangkok Declaration. The original five founding members were Indonesia, Malaysia, the Philippines, Singapore, and Thailand. The organization later expanded to include Brunei Darussalam, Viet Nam, Lao PDR, Myanmar, Cambodia, and Timor-Leste.

ASEAN’s creation reflected the historical conditions of Southeast Asia in the 1960s. The region was shaped by decolonization, Cold War competition, internal conflict, ideological rivalry, border tensions, and development challenges. Newly independent states wanted stability and space for national development.

The founders of ASEAN wanted to avoid repeated conflict among neighbors and reduce external manipulation by major powers. They also wanted to build confidence, promote economic and social cooperation, and create a regional identity.

ASEAN began as a modest grouping focused on peace, cooperation, and confidence-building. Over time, it became a wider regional community with political-security, economic, and socio-cultural pillars. It also became the center of wider regional forums that include external partners such as China, Japan, South Korea, the United States, Australia, India, New Zealand, the European Union, and others.

ASEAN’s development can be understood in stages:

1. **Founding and confidence-building** — establishing trust among original members.
2. **Expansion** — bringing more Southeast Asian states into the organization.
3. **Institutionalization** — adopting the ASEAN Charter and creating a more formal legal framework.
4. **Community-building** — organizing cooperation under political-security, economic, and socio-cultural pillars.
5. **Regional architecture** — convening wider forums such as the ASEAN Regional Forum, East Asia Summit, ASEAN Plus Three, and ADMM-Plus.
6. **Post-2025 planning** — moving toward ASEAN Community Vision 2045 and new strategic plans.

ASEAN’s history shows that regional governance often grows gradually. ASEAN did not begin as a highly integrated institution. It developed through trust-building, repeated meetings, and practical cooperation.

## The ASEAN Charter

The ASEAN Charter is ASEAN’s foundational legal and institutional document. It entered into force in 2008 and gave ASEAN a stronger legal personality, clearer institutional structure, and formal statement of purposes and principles.

The Charter matters because it explains what ASEAN is trying to do and how its members are expected to behave.

### Purposes of ASEAN Under the Charter

The ASEAN Charter identifies several purposes, including:

- maintaining and enhancing peace, security, and stability
- strengthening democracy, good governance, and the rule of law
- promoting and protecting human rights and fundamental freedoms
- creating a single market and production base
- reducing poverty and narrowing the development gap
- promoting sustainable development
- enhancing regional resilience
- preserving Southeast Asia as a nuclear weapon-free zone
- responding to common challenges
- promoting people-oriented and people-centered cooperation

Student-friendly explanation: **the Charter gives ASEAN a rulebook for cooperation and a legal identity as a regional organization.**

### Principles of ASEAN Under the Charter

The Charter also includes principles such as:

- respect for sovereignty
- equality among member states
- territorial integrity
- non-interference in internal affairs
- peaceful settlement of disputes
- renunciation of aggression and threat or use of force
- respect for international law
- consultation and consensus
- adherence to multilateral trade rules
- effective cooperation

These principles help ASEAN maintain unity among diverse states. However, they also create limits. For example, non-interference can make it difficult to respond to domestic crises inside member states, while consensus can slow regional action when members disagree.

## Core Principles of ASEAN

ASEAN is shaped by principles that reflect Southeast Asia’s historical experiences and political diversity.

### Respect for Sovereignty

ASEAN respects the sovereignty of its member states. This means each state remains legally independent and controls its own domestic affairs. ASEAN does not replace national governments.

Respect for sovereignty helps members feel safe in the organization. Smaller states can participate without fearing that larger states will dominate them formally. However, sovereignty also limits enforcement because ASEAN usually cannot force states to act against their will.

### Non-Interference

Non-interference means states should not interfere in one another’s internal affairs. This principle developed partly because Southeast Asian states wanted to avoid destabilizing each other after decolonization and conflict.

Non-interference can reduce conflict among neighbors, but it can also limit ASEAN’s response to human rights crises, coups, civil conflict, or domestic instability with regional effects.

### Peaceful Settlement of Disputes

ASEAN emphasizes peaceful settlement of disputes. This means members should use negotiation, diplomacy, mediation, consultation, and other peaceful methods rather than force.

This principle is reflected in the ASEAN Charter, the Treaty of Amity and Cooperation, and ASEAN’s approach to regional diplomacy.

### Renunciation of the Threat or Use of Force

ASEAN principles reject the threat or use of force as a way to settle disputes. This supports regional peace and stability.

### Consultation and Consensus

ASEAN often makes decisions through consultation and consensus. This means members discuss issues until they find a position all can accept or at least not oppose. Consensus protects unity but may also produce slow or cautious outcomes.

### Regional Cooperation

ASEAN emphasizes cooperation across many fields, including security, economics, health, education, culture, disaster response, environment, and development.

### Respect for International Law

ASEAN documents often refer to international law, including the UN Charter and, in maritime contexts, UNCLOS. This supports the idea of a rules-based regional order.

These principles are both ASEAN’s strength and its limitation. They help maintain peace among diverse states, but they can weaken rapid or forceful action.

## The ASEAN Way

The “ASEAN Way” is a term often used to describe ASEAN’s style of diplomacy and decision-making. It generally refers to consultation, consensus, informality, non-confrontation, respect for sovereignty, non-interference, and gradual cooperation.

The ASEAN Way developed because Southeast Asian states were diverse, newly independent, and cautious about external interference and regional conflict. Instead of building a highly centralized organization, ASEAN built trust through regular meetings, personal diplomacy, quiet consultation, and avoidance of public confrontation.

### Strengths of the ASEAN Way

The ASEAN Way has several strengths:

- It reduces the risk of open conflict among members.
- It helps diverse states cooperate despite political differences.
- It avoids forcing states into public confrontation.
- It preserves regional unity.
- It allows gradual trust-building.
- It helps smaller states feel protected from domination.
- It supports dialogue even during disagreement.

This style has helped ASEAN survive for decades despite major differences among members.

### Limits of the ASEAN Way

The ASEAN Way is also criticized because it can cause:

- slow decision-making
- weak enforcement
- watered-down statements
- difficulty responding to crises
- avoidance of sensitive issues
- protection of governments from criticism
- failure to respond strongly to human rights abuses
- lack of clear accountability

The ASEAN Way is therefore not simply good or bad. It is a diplomatic style that helps maintain unity but can limit decisive action.

A balanced explanation is: **the ASEAN Way helps ASEAN stay together, but it can also make ASEAN slow and weak when stronger action is needed.**

## Consultation and Consensus

Consultation means discussion among members before decisions are made. Consensus means a decision is adopted only when members can accept it, or at least do not object strongly enough to block it.

ASEAN uses consensus because its members are diverse and protective of sovereignty. Consensus ensures that decisions are not imposed on unwilling members.

### Why Consensus Helps

Consensus helps ASEAN because:

- it protects smaller states
- it preserves unity
- it avoids public division
- it encourages compromise
- it prevents domination by one member
- it builds trust over time

### Why Consensus Limits ASEAN

Consensus can also limit ASEAN because:

- one or a few members can slow action
- statements may become vague
- urgent responses may be delayed
- controversial issues may be avoided
- strong action may become impossible
- external powers may influence individual members and weaken common positions

South China Sea diplomacy shows this clearly. ASEAN members do not all have the same relationship with China or the same level of concern about maritime disputes. This makes it difficult for ASEAN to adopt strong unified positions.

Consensus is therefore both protective and limiting. It protects unity but can create paralysis.

## Non-Interference

Non-interference is the principle that states should not intervene in one another’s internal affairs. It is one of ASEAN’s most important and most debated principles.

### Why ASEAN Values Non-Interference

ASEAN values non-interference because:

- members want to protect sovereignty
- many states experienced colonialism or external intervention
- governments want stability
- public criticism can damage trust
- domestic conflicts can become regional disputes
- members have different political systems

Non-interference helped ASEAN maintain stable relations among members during periods when Southeast Asia faced internal conflicts and ideological divisions.

### Limits of Non-Interference

Non-interference is criticized because it can make ASEAN cautious or weak when domestic problems have regional effects. These problems may include:

- human rights abuses
- military coups
- civil conflict
- refugee flows
- transnational crime
- humanitarian crises
- public health crises
- environmental damage
- political repression

The Myanmar crisis is a major example. ASEAN has tried to respond through diplomacy and the Five-Point Consensus, but implementation has been weak. Non-interference and consensus make it difficult for ASEAN to pressure a member state strongly.

A balanced explanation is: **non-interference helps prevent conflict among governments, but it can also limit action when people inside a member state face serious harm.**

## Regional Governance vs Global Governance

Regional governance refers to cooperation among states and actors within a specific region. It is different from global governance, which involves cooperation at the worldwide level.

### Regional Governance

Regional governance focuses on shared regional problems. These may include:

- cross-border trade
- regional security
- migration
- health
- disasters
- pollution
- infrastructure
- maritime issues
- regional identity
- economic integration
- transnational crime

Regional governance is useful because neighboring states often share geography and practical concerns. They may understand local conditions better than global institutions do.

### Global Governance

Global governance deals with worldwide problems such as climate change, pandemics, trade, human rights, international law, and peace and security. It involves global institutions such as the United Nations, World Trade Organization, World Health Organization, International Monetary Fund, World Bank, and international courts.

### ASEAN as Regional Governance

ASEAN is a regional governance organization within the larger global governance system. It connects local, national, regional, and global levels.

For example:

- ASEAN supports regional disaster response while working with the United Nations and humanitarian partners.
- ASEAN discusses the South China Sea while referring to international law and UNCLOS.
- ASEAN supports regional economic integration while participating in wider trade arrangements such as RCEP.
- ASEAN addresses health cooperation while connecting to global public health frameworks.
- ASEAN-led forums bring external powers into Southeast Asian regional dialogue.

ASEAN shows that regional governance can serve as a bridge between national governments and global institutions.

## ASEAN’s Three Community Pillars

ASEAN organizes much of its cooperation through three community pillars:

1. ASEAN Political-Security Community
2. ASEAN Economic Community
3. ASEAN Socio-Cultural Community

These pillars were created to organize ASEAN’s community-building process across major issue areas. They show that ASEAN is not only about security or only about trade. It tries to build a broad regional community.

### Why ASEAN Uses Community-Building

ASEAN uses community-building because cooperation is gradual. Instead of creating one centralized authority, ASEAN builds habits of cooperation across different sectors. Over time, these repeated interactions can deepen regional identity, trust, and institutional capacity.

The three pillars also show that regional stability depends on more than military security. Economic development, social welfare, education, environment, health, and people-to-people ties also matter.

## ASEAN Political-Security Community

The ASEAN Political-Security Community, or APSC, focuses on peace, security, political cooperation, regional stability, and shared norms.

The APSC is not a military alliance. ASEAN members do not have a NATO-style collective defense system. Instead, the APSC emphasizes dialogue, confidence-building, peaceful settlement, cooperation against transnational threats, and respect for international law.

### Areas of Cooperation

The APSC covers areas such as:

- conflict prevention
- peaceful settlement of disputes
- defense dialogue
- transnational crime
- counterterrorism cooperation
- maritime security
- cybersecurity
- disaster-related security cooperation
- confidence-building measures
- regional norms
- preventive diplomacy
- nuclear weapon-free zone commitments

### Strengths

The APSC contributes to regional governance by:

- providing regular political and security dialogue
- creating habits of communication
- supporting confidence-building
- reducing mistrust among members
- giving members platforms to discuss sensitive issues
- connecting ASEAN to wider security forums
- supporting peaceful settlement of disputes

### Limitations

The APSC has limits:

- weak enforcement tools
- consensus constraints
- sensitivity around national security
- major-power rivalry
- difficulty responding to the South China Sea
- difficulty responding to Myanmar
- reluctance to criticize members directly

A simple explanation is: **the APSC helps ASEAN talk about security and build trust, but it is not a strong enforcement or defense system.**

## ASEAN Economic Community

The ASEAN Economic Community, or AEC, focuses on regional economic integration. Its goal is to make ASEAN more connected, competitive, innovative, and integrated.

### Areas of Cooperation

The AEC includes cooperation on:

- trade in goods
- trade in services
- investment
- customs cooperation
- standards
- supply chains
- competition policy
- consumer protection
- intellectual property
- transport and connectivity
- digital economy
- finance
- energy
- small and medium enterprises
- narrowing development gaps

ASEAN economic integration supports regional development by reducing barriers, improving connectivity, and making Southeast Asia more attractive for investment and production networks.

### Why Economic Integration Matters

Economic cooperation can support peace by increasing interdependence. States that trade and invest with one another have more reasons to maintain stability. Economic integration also helps ASEAN compete globally as a region.

ASEAN has developed trade arrangements and has played a central role in RCEP, the Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership. RCEP connects ASEAN with major regional partners and builds on ASEAN’s existing free trade agreements.

### Limitations

The AEC faces challenges:

- development gaps among members
- non-tariff barriers
- uneven implementation
- infrastructure gaps
- domestic protectionism
- different regulatory systems
- different economic capacities
- limited labor mobility
- political sensitivity around markets and jobs

Economic integration is real but incomplete. ASEAN is not a single market like the European Union. National regulations, borders, and domestic politics still matter.

## ASEAN Socio-Cultural Community

The ASEAN Socio-Cultural Community, or ASCC, focuses on people-centered cooperation. It aims to improve quality of life, social welfare, education, health, culture, environment, disaster resilience, and sustainable development.

### Areas of Cooperation

The ASCC covers:

- education
- public health
- labor
- social protection
- culture
- youth
- sports
- disaster management
- environment
- climate resilience
- sustainable development
- poverty reduction
- narrowing development gaps
- women and children’s welfare
- migrant worker concerns
- people-to-people exchanges

### Why Socio-Cultural Cooperation Matters

Socio-cultural cooperation matters because regional governance is not only about governments. It also affects people’s daily lives. Health crises, disasters, education, labor mobility, pollution, and climate change directly affect communities.

The ASCC supports the idea that ASEAN should be people-oriented and people-centered, not only state-centered.

### Limitations

The ASCC faces limits:

- funding constraints
- capacity gaps
- uneven implementation
- limited public awareness of ASEAN
- different domestic policies
- weak enforcement
- development gaps among member states

A simple explanation is: **the ASCC is the ASEAN pillar most focused on people, welfare, culture, health, environment, and resilience.**

## ASEAN Centrality

ASEAN centrality means ASEAN seeks to remain at the center of regional diplomacy and institution-building in Southeast Asia and the wider Indo-Pacific. It means ASEAN tries to be the hub around which regional forums and dialogue mechanisms are organized.

ASEAN centrality does not mean ASEAN controls all regional powers. It means ASEAN tries to convene, shape, and lead regional dialogue so that Southeast Asian states have a collective voice.

### Why ASEAN Centrality Matters

ASEAN centrality matters because Southeast Asia is surrounded by major powers and strategic competition. If ASEAN is divided or weak, external powers may shape the region without considering Southeast Asian interests.

ASEAN centrality helps:

- give Southeast Asian states collective diplomatic space
- prevent domination by one major power
- maintain inclusive dialogue platforms
- keep regional institutions open to multiple partners
- support a balance of relationships
- allow small and medium states to influence regional architecture

### Limits of ASEAN Centrality

ASEAN centrality has limits:

- it depends on ASEAN unity
- it depends on credibility
- major-power rivalry can pressure members
- internal divisions weaken ASEAN’s voice
- external powers may support ASEAN centrality rhetorically but act around it in practice
- ASEAN-led forums may produce dialogue but not enforcement

A useful explanation is: **ASEAN centrality is strongest when ASEAN is united and credible; it is weakest when members are divided.**

## ASEAN-Led Regional Forums

ASEAN convenes several wider regional forums. These forums bring ASEAN members together with external partners. They are important because ASEAN’s influence often comes from convening power.

### ASEAN Regional Forum

The ASEAN Regional Forum, or ARF, is a major security dialogue forum. It focuses on confidence-building, preventive diplomacy, and constructive dialogue on political and security issues in the Asia-Pacific.

The ARF is not an enforcement body. It is mainly a forum for dialogue, transparency, confidence-building, and preventive diplomacy.

### East Asia Summit

The East Asia Summit, or EAS, is a leaders-led forum involving ASEAN and key external partners. It addresses strategic, political, and economic issues. It is important for regional architecture because it includes major powers in a forum convened around ASEAN.

### ASEAN Defence Ministers’ Meeting

The ASEAN Defence Ministers’ Meeting, or ADMM, is the main ASEAN defense cooperation mechanism among ASEAN defense ministers. It supports defense dialogue and cooperation among ASEAN members.

### ADMM-Plus

ADMM-Plus includes ASEAN defense ministers and dialogue partners. It supports practical defense cooperation in areas such as maritime security, counterterrorism, humanitarian assistance, disaster relief, peacekeeping operations, military medicine, and cybersecurity.

### ASEAN Plus Three

ASEAN Plus Three includes ASEAN, China, Japan, and South Korea. It supports cooperation in finance, economics, food security, health, education, and other areas.

### Why These Forums Matter

ASEAN-led forums contribute to regional governance by:

- keeping communication channels open
- reducing misunderstanding
- building confidence
- involving major powers in dialogue
- supporting cooperation on practical issues
- reinforcing ASEAN centrality

Their limit is that they usually rely on dialogue and cooperation rather than strict enforcement.

## Treaty of Amity and Cooperation

The Treaty of Amity and Cooperation in Southeast Asia, or TAC, is one of ASEAN’s most important norm-setting instruments. It was signed in 1976 and sets out principles for peaceful relations among states in Southeast Asia.

### Core Principles

The TAC includes principles such as:

- mutual respect for independence, sovereignty, equality, territorial integrity, and national identity
- the right of every state to be free from external interference, subversion, or coercion
- non-interference in internal affairs
- peaceful settlement of disputes
- renunciation of the threat or use of force
- effective cooperation

### Why the TAC Matters

The TAC matters because it expresses ASEAN’s vision of regional order: states should cooperate, respect sovereignty, avoid force, and settle disputes peacefully.

External partners accede to the TAC to show that they accept ASEAN’s regional norms and want constructive relations with ASEAN. This strengthens ASEAN’s role as a norm-setting regional organization.

### Limits of the TAC

The TAC is important as a norm-setting instrument, but it is not a strong enforcement system. Its main power is diplomatic and normative. It supports expectations of peaceful behavior, but it does not automatically prevent disputes.

## ASEAN and the South China Sea

The South China Sea is one of the biggest tests of ASEAN regional governance. Several ASEAN member states are claimants, including the Philippines, Viet Nam, Malaysia, and Brunei. Other ASEAN members are not direct claimants but still care about regional stability, freedom of navigation, trade, and ASEAN unity.

### Why ASEAN Unity Is Difficult

ASEAN unity is difficult because:

- not all members are claimants
- members have different relationships with China
- economic ties with China vary
- threat perceptions differ
- consensus is required
- some states prefer quiet diplomacy
- others want stronger statements
- external major-power rivalry complicates the issue

### ASEAN’s Role

ASEAN’s role includes:

- providing a diplomatic platform
- promoting peaceful settlement
- supporting self-restraint
- negotiating with China
- discussing a Code of Conduct
- maintaining regional dialogue
- referring to international law and UNCLOS in careful language
- trying to prevent escalation

### Limits

ASEAN has not functioned as an enforcement body in the South China Sea. It cannot directly enforce the 2016 arbitral award. It has struggled to issue strong unified statements when members disagree.

The South China Sea shows ASEAN’s strengths and weaknesses clearly: it can keep dialogue open, but it has limited power to compel behavior.

## Declaration on the Conduct of Parties in the South China Sea

The Declaration on the Conduct of Parties in the South China Sea, often called the DOC, was adopted by ASEAN member states and China in 2002. It is an important diplomatic framework for managing the South China Sea issue.

### Main Emphases

The DOC emphasizes:

- peaceful settlement of disputes
- self-restraint
- confidence-building
- cooperation
- respect for international law
- working toward a Code of Conduct

The DOC calls on parties to resolve disputes by peaceful means without resorting to the threat or use of force.

### Strengths

The DOC has strengths:

- it provides a diplomatic framework
- it affirms peaceful handling of disputes
- it supports regional dialogue
- it creates a reference point for ASEAN-China engagement
- it encourages self-restraint
- it keeps the idea of a Code of Conduct alive

### Limitations

The DOC also has limitations:

- weak enforcement
- limited legal force compared with a treaty
- continued incidents despite the declaration
- vague wording
- no strong compliance mechanism
- slow progress toward a binding Code of Conduct

The DOC shows how ASEAN often works: it builds diplomatic frameworks, but enforcement remains difficult.

## Code of Conduct Negotiations

The South China Sea Code of Conduct, or COC, is intended to be a more developed framework for managing behavior in the South China Sea. ASEAN and China have discussed it for many years.

### What the Code of Conduct Is Intended to Do

A Code of Conduct could help:

- reduce incidents at sea
- improve crisis management
- support self-restraint
- clarify behavior rules
- reduce risk of miscalculation
- strengthen confidence-building
- support cooperation
- manage tensions

### Key Issues in Negotiations

Key issues include:

- whether the COC should be legally binding
- geographic scope
- dispute management procedures
- enforcement mechanisms
- relationship to UNCLOS
- whether external powers are mentioned
- rules on military activities
- resource development
- coast guard conduct
- implementation and monitoring

### Why Negotiations Are Slow

Negotiations are slow because ASEAN members and China have different interests. Even among ASEAN members, priorities differ. Some states want stronger legal language, while others prefer flexibility. China may prefer language that limits external involvement, while some ASEAN members want to protect partnerships and freedom of navigation.

A Code of Conduct could help manage behavior, but it would not automatically solve sovereignty disputes or eliminate power politics.

## ASEAN and the West Philippine Sea / South China Sea Case

The Philippines is an ASEAN member and one of the claimant states in the South China Sea. It uses UNCLOS and the 2016 arbitral award as part of its legal position in the West Philippine Sea.

ASEAN connects to the West Philippine Sea / South China Sea case because the dispute affects regional stability, maritime security, and ASEAN-China relations. However, ASEAN as a whole has not acted as an enforcement body for the 2016 arbitral award.

### Why ASEAN Has Not Enforced the Award

ASEAN has not directly enforced the award because:

- ASEAN is not a court or enforcement body
- the award was between the Philippines and China
- ASEAN operates by consensus
- not all ASEAN members are claimants
- some members are cautious about confronting China
- ASEAN has limited coercive tools
- South China Sea diplomacy is tied to major-power rivalry

### Why ASEAN Still Matters

ASEAN still matters because:

- it provides a diplomatic platform
- it keeps communication open
- it supports regional stability
- it engages China collectively
- it discusses confidence-building
- it pursues a Code of Conduct
- it gives the Philippines and other members a regional setting for diplomacy

ASEAN is therefore not an enforcement solution, but it remains a regional governance platform.

## ASEAN and International Law

ASEAN’s relationship with international law is careful and pragmatic. ASEAN documents frequently refer to the UN Charter, international law, peaceful settlement of disputes, and the rule of law. In maritime issues, ASEAN statements often refer to UNCLOS.

ASEAN supports a rules-based regional order, but it often avoids direct confrontation. This reflects ASEAN’s preference for consensus, diplomacy, and non-confrontational language.

### Legal Instruments and Norms

ASEAN’s legal and normative framework includes:

- ASEAN Charter
- Treaty of Amity and Cooperation
- ASEAN human rights documents
- regional agreements on trade, haze, disaster management, and other issues
- references to UNCLOS in maritime contexts
- declarations and plans of action
- community blueprints and strategic plans

### Soft Law and Hard Law

ASEAN often uses soft-law instruments such as declarations, plans, guidelines, and statements. These may not always be legally binding in the same way as treaties, but they can still shape expectations and cooperation.

ASEAN also has legally binding agreements, such as the ASEAN Charter and the ASEAN Agreement on Transboundary Haze Pollution. However, even legally binding agreements may face implementation challenges.

## ASEAN and Major-Power Rivalry

ASEAN operates in a region affected by major-power competition, especially involving China and the United States, but also Japan, Australia, India, South Korea, the European Union, and others.

### ASEAN’s Strategy

ASEAN generally tries to avoid choosing sides. It seeks to maintain relations with multiple partners while preserving autonomy and centrality. This approach allows ASEAN members to benefit from trade, investment, security cooperation, and diplomacy with many partners.

### Why Rivalry Increases ASEAN’s Importance

Major-power rivalry increases ASEAN’s importance because Southeast Asia becomes a key region for diplomacy, trade, security, technology, and maritime strategy. External powers often seek ASEAN support or engagement.

### Why Rivalry Exposes ASEAN’s Divisions

Major-power rivalry also exposes ASEAN’s divisions because member states have different relationships with external powers. Some may be more economically dependent on China. Others may have defense ties with the United States. Some prefer neutrality, while others seek stronger external support.

ASEAN centrality is challenged when external powers bypass ASEAN or when ASEAN members cannot agree on a common position.

## ASEAN and Economic Regionalism

Economic regionalism refers to regional economic cooperation and integration. ASEAN has been a major driver of economic regionalism in Southeast Asia.

### ASEAN Free Trade Area

The ASEAN Free Trade Area supports trade liberalization among members. It helped reduce tariffs and encouraged regional supply chains.

### ASEAN Economic Community

The ASEAN Economic Community deepens regional economic cooperation and aims to create a more integrated and competitive region.

### Trade Facilitation

ASEAN works on customs procedures, standards, regulatory cooperation, and trade facilitation to make cross-border trade easier.

### Supply Chains

ASEAN is part of regional and global supply chains. Cooperation helps attract investment and support manufacturing networks.

### Investment

ASEAN seeks to create a more attractive investment environment through agreements and cooperation.

### Connectivity

Connectivity includes physical infrastructure, transport links, digital connectivity, energy connections, and people-to-people movement.

### Digital Economy

ASEAN increasingly focuses on digital trade, e-commerce, cybersecurity, and digital transformation.

### RCEP

RCEP is a regional free trade agreement involving ASEAN and five ASEAN free trade partners: China, Japan, South Korea, Australia, and New Zealand. It builds on ASEAN-centered economic architecture and supports wider regional trade integration.

### Limits

ASEAN economic regionalism faces limits:

- development gaps
- infrastructure gaps
- regulatory differences
- non-tariff barriers
- domestic protectionism
- uneven implementation
- limited labor mobility
- different national priorities

Economic cooperation is one of ASEAN’s strongest areas, but it remains incomplete.

## ASEAN and Disaster Response

Southeast Asia is highly exposed to disasters, including typhoons, earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, floods, landslides, tsunamis, droughts, haze, and climate-related extreme weather. Disaster response is therefore a major area of regional governance.

### ASEAN Cooperation on Disaster Management

ASEAN disaster cooperation includes regional coordination, information sharing, preparedness, emergency response, and humanitarian assistance. The AHA Centre, the ASEAN Coordinating Centre for Humanitarian Assistance on disaster management, facilitates cooperation and coordination among ASEAN member states and with the United Nations and international organizations.

### Strengths

ASEAN disaster governance has strengths:

- regional coordination
- rapid information sharing
- practical cooperation
- humanitarian response mechanisms
- disaster preparedness
- connection with international partners
- focus on Southeast Asian conditions

### Limits

It also has limits:

- funding constraints
- state consent and access
- varying national capacities
- infrastructure gaps
- speed of disasters
- climate change increasing risk
- difficulty coordinating many actors

Disaster response shows ASEAN at its most practical. It also shows why regional institutions need resources and cooperation from member states.

## ASEAN and Public Health

Public health is an important area of regional governance because diseases cross borders. Southeast Asia’s mobility, tourism, trade, and dense urban areas make health cooperation important.

### Areas of Cooperation

ASEAN public health cooperation includes:

- disease surveillance
- pandemic response
- health information sharing
- cross-border coordination
- health emergency preparedness
- vaccine access discussions
- health systems cooperation
- cooperation with ASEAN Plus Three partners

The COVID-19 pandemic showed the need for regional health cooperation, supply chain coordination, and information sharing.

### Strengths

ASEAN can support:

- early warning
- coordination among health ministries
- regional dialogue
- sharing of best practices
- cooperation with global health institutions
- health security planning

### Limits

Limits include:

- different national health systems
- uneven capacity
- domestic politics
- border control differences
- vaccine access inequality
- resource shortages
- reliance on national implementation

Public health governance shows that regional cooperation matters, but actual response depends heavily on national capacity and political decisions.

## ASEAN and Migration / Labor Mobility

Migration and labor mobility are major issues in Southeast Asia. Millions of people move across borders for work, safety, family, or opportunity. ASEAN includes both labor-sending and labor-receiving countries.

### Migration Issues

ASEAN migration issues include:

- migrant workers
- domestic workers
- undocumented migration
- trafficking in persons
- refugees and displaced persons
- border control
- remittances
- labor rights
- recruitment practices
- irregular migration by sea

### ASEAN Cooperation

ASEAN cooperation includes discussions on migrant worker protection, transnational crime, trafficking, and labor standards. However, migration remains sensitive because states closely guard control over borders and labor markets.

### Limits

Migration governance faces limits:

- sovereignty over borders
- different labor laws
- weak enforcement
- informal recruitment networks
- exploitation of migrant workers
- human rights concerns
- limited protection for irregular migrants
- political sensitivity around refugees

Migration shows how regional governance must balance state sovereignty with human security.

## ASEAN and Human Rights

ASEAN’s human rights framework is one of its most debated areas. ASEAN has adopted the ASEAN Human Rights Declaration and established the ASEAN Intergovernmental Commission on Human Rights, or AICHR.

### ASEAN Human Rights Declaration

The ASEAN Human Rights Declaration affirms civil, political, economic, social, and cultural rights, as well as rights related to development and peace. It reflects ASEAN’s attempt to develop a regional human rights framework.

### ASEAN Intergovernmental Commission on Human Rights

AICHR is ASEAN’s human rights body. It promotes human rights cooperation, awareness, and dialogue. However, it has limited enforcement power.

### Criticisms

ASEAN’s human rights system is criticized because:

- enforcement is weak
- AICHR has limited independence
- non-interference limits action
- governments control representation
- victims may have limited access
- ASEAN struggles to respond to abuses
- consensus limits strong action

### Balanced View

ASEAN’s human rights framework matters because it creates regional language and institutions for human rights. But it remains weaker than many human rights advocates want. Human rights are one of the clearest areas where ASEAN’s sovereignty-respecting model faces criticism.

## ASEAN and Environment / Climate Governance

Southeast Asia faces major environmental and climate challenges. These include:

- climate change
- sea-level rise
- typhoons and floods
- drought
- biodiversity loss
- haze pollution
- deforestation
- marine pollution
- plastic waste
- coastal vulnerability
- disaster risk
- water management

### ASEAN Environmental Cooperation

ASEAN cooperates on environmental protection, climate resilience, biodiversity, disaster risk reduction, haze, marine pollution, and sustainable development.

### Transboundary Haze

The ASEAN Agreement on Transboundary Haze Pollution is an important environmental agreement. It aims to prevent and monitor haze pollution caused by land and forest fires through national efforts and regional cooperation.

Haze is a classic transboundary issue because fires in one country can create health and environmental problems in neighboring countries.

### Limits

Environmental governance faces limits:

- national development priorities
- weak enforcement
- land-use conflicts
- agricultural and plantation interests
- capacity gaps
- funding limits
- climate vulnerability
- uneven implementation

Climate and environmental issues show why ASEAN cooperation is necessary, but also why domestic policy and enforcement remain crucial.

## ASEAN and Myanmar

Myanmar is one of the most serious challenges for ASEAN regional governance. The crisis after the 2021 military coup created political violence, humanitarian suffering, displacement, and regional instability.

ASEAN responded with the Five-Point Consensus in 2021. The consensus called for an immediate cessation of violence, constructive dialogue among all parties, mediation by a special envoy, humanitarian assistance through ASEAN mechanisms, and a visit by the special envoy to meet all parties concerned.

### Why Myanmar Tests ASEAN

Myanmar tests ASEAN because it involves:

- non-interference
- human rights
- political violence
- humanitarian crisis
- refugee and displacement effects
- consensus decision-making
- ASEAN credibility
- relations with external powers

### Implementation Problems

Implementation has been difficult because:

- violence has continued
- parties have not reached inclusive dialogue
- access for humanitarian assistance has been limited
- ASEAN members differ in preferred approaches
- the military authorities have resisted pressure
- ASEAN has limited coercive tools

Myanmar shows both the need for ASEAN and the limits of ASEAN. ASEAN provides a diplomatic platform, but its enforcement capacity is weak.

## Strengths of ASEAN Regional Governance

ASEAN has several important strengths.

### Regular Dialogue

ASEAN provides regular meetings among leaders, ministers, officials, and sectoral bodies. This keeps communication open even during disagreement.

### Reduces Risk of Conflict Among Members

ASEAN has helped create habits of peaceful interaction among Southeast Asian states. It does not eliminate disputes, but it reduces the likelihood of open conflict among members.

### Gives Southeast Asia a Collective Voice

ASEAN allows Southeast Asian states to speak collectively in regional and global forums.

### Supports Economic Integration

ASEAN has helped reduce trade barriers, promote investment, and connect regional economies.

### Creates Regional Norms

ASEAN promotes norms such as non-interference, peaceful settlement, consultation, consensus, and regional cooperation.

### Convenes Wider Regional Forums

ASEAN’s convening power brings major powers into ASEAN-led forums, supporting ASEAN centrality.

### Supports Confidence-Building

ASEAN mechanisms encourage dialogue and trust-building among states.

### Encourages Peaceful Settlement

ASEAN emphasizes peaceful settlement of disputes and rejection of force.

### Helps Manage Major-Power Competition

ASEAN provides a platform where major powers can engage Southeast Asia through dialogue rather than only rivalry.

### Supports Practical Cooperation

ASEAN supports cooperation on disasters, health, development, environment, labor, transnational crime, and education.

ASEAN matters even without strong enforcement power because it provides structure, communication, trust, and diplomatic space.

## Limits and Criticisms of ASEAN

ASEAN also faces serious limits and criticisms.

### Consensus Can Slow Action

Consensus protects unity but can delay or weaken decisions.

### Non-Interference Can Limit Crisis Response

Non-interference can make it difficult to respond to human rights abuses, coups, or domestic conflicts with regional effects.

### Weak Enforcement Mechanisms

ASEAN often relies on voluntary implementation, diplomacy, and peer pressure rather than coercive enforcement.

### Institutional Capacity Limits

ASEAN has many meetings and commitments, but the ASEAN Secretariat and regional bodies may have limited resources relative to the size of ASEAN’s agenda.

### Vague Commitments

ASEAN documents sometimes use broad or cautious language, which can make implementation difficult.

### Uneven Implementation

Member states may implement agreements at different speeds due to capacity, politics, or national priorities.

### Limited Public Awareness

Many ordinary citizens may not understand ASEAN’s work, which weakens public connection to regional governance.

### Divisions Over China and the South China Sea

Member states differ in their claims, economic ties, and strategic relationships with China.

### Difficulty Addressing Myanmar

Myanmar shows ASEAN’s difficulty in responding to domestic crises that have regional consequences.

### Gap Between Declarations and Action

ASEAN often produces declarations and plans, but implementation may lag behind.

These limits do not make ASEAN useless. They show that ASEAN is a cautious, sovereignty-respecting regional organization with limited enforcement tools.

## ASEAN as a Case of Regional Governance

ASEAN teaches several lessons about regional governance.

### Regional Organizations Can Manage Shared Problems

ASEAN helps states cooperate on trade, security, disasters, health, environment, migration, and diplomacy.

### Sovereignty Remains Powerful

ASEAN respects state sovereignty. This helps members cooperate but limits enforcement.

### Consensus Preserves Unity but Limits Action

Consensus prevents domination by one member but can produce slow or weak responses.

### Regional Diplomacy Reduces Conflict but Does Not Always Solve Disputes

Dialogue can manage tensions, but it may not solve difficult disputes such as the South China Sea.

### Economic Integration Can Deepen Cooperation

Trade and investment ties can increase interdependence and support regional development.

### Regional Organizations May Struggle with Enforcement

ASEAN can create norms and frameworks, but it often struggles to force compliance.

ASEAN shows that regional governance is not about total control. It is about building cooperation among sovereign states.

## ASEAN, Global Governance, and the Enforcement Gap

ASEAN connects closely to the enforcement gap. Many ASEAN agreements, declarations, and statements depend on voluntary implementation, consensus, and political will. ASEAN often relies on diplomacy and soft pressure rather than coercive enforcement.

### South China Sea

In the South China Sea, ASEAN supports dialogue, the DOC, and Code of Conduct negotiations. However, ASEAN does not directly enforce the 2016 arbitral award and has difficulty adopting strong positions because members differ.

### Myanmar

In Myanmar, ASEAN adopted the Five-Point Consensus, but implementation has been weak. This shows the gap between regional agreement and actual behavior.

### Haze Pollution

ASEAN has a transboundary haze agreement, but haze remains difficult because enforcement depends on national land-use policy, corporate behavior, local enforcement, and weather conditions.

### Human Rights

ASEAN has human rights documents and institutions, but enforcement is limited by non-interference, consensus, and weak mechanisms.

ASEAN is still useful despite enforcement limits because it provides norms, dialogue, crisis channels, cooperation mechanisms, and regional identity. The enforcement gap does not make ASEAN irrelevant. It shows why regional governance requires patience, pressure, and institutional strengthening.

## Balanced Evaluation

Students should avoid two extreme views.

One extreme is saying ASEAN is useless because it is slow and weak in enforcement. This ignores ASEAN’s contribution to regional peace, dialogue, economic integration, and cooperation.

The other extreme is saying ASEAN can solve every regional problem. This overstates ASEAN’s power and ignores sovereignty, consensus, non-interference, and unequal interests.

ASEAN is best understood as a sovereignty-respecting regional governance organization. Its strengths and weaknesses come from the same features:

- consensus
- non-interference
- gradualism
- informality
- regional unity
- respect for sovereignty
- quiet diplomacy

These features help ASEAN stay together, but they also limit strong collective action.

A balanced explanation is: **ASEAN is limited but important. It cannot enforce everything, but it keeps Southeast Asian cooperation alive and gives the region a collective diplomatic platform.**

## Common Misconceptions

### “ASEAN is a country.”

Incorrect. ASEAN is a regional organization made up of sovereign states.

### “ASEAN is like the European Union.”

Not exactly. ASEAN is much less supranational than the European Union. ASEAN does not have the same centralized legal and political authority.

### “ASEAN can force all members to obey.”

Incorrect. ASEAN has limited enforcement power and usually works through consensus, diplomacy, and cooperation.

### “ASEAN controls the South China Sea.”

Incorrect. ASEAN does not control the South China Sea. Some ASEAN members are claimants, and ASEAN provides a diplomatic platform.

### “ASEAN has no power because it uses consensus.”

Incorrect. Consensus limits ASEAN, but ASEAN still has convening power, legitimacy, and diplomatic influence.

### “Consensus means every member always agrees strongly.”

Incorrect. Consensus often means members can accept a decision or choose not to block it. It does not mean every member is equally enthusiastic.

### “Non-interference means ASEAN never discusses internal problems.”

Incorrect. ASEAN may discuss internal crises when they affect regional stability, but its response is often cautious.

### “ASEAN can easily solve the West Philippine Sea dispute.”

Incorrect. ASEAN is limited by consensus, different member interests, China’s role, and the dispute’s legal and strategic complexity.

### “ASEAN is only about economics.”

Incorrect. ASEAN also works on security, diplomacy, health, disasters, environment, human rights, migration, and culture.

### “ASEAN is only about security.”

Incorrect. ASEAN has economic and socio-cultural pillars as well.

### “ASEAN centrality means ASEAN controls all regional powers.”

Incorrect. ASEAN centrality means ASEAN tries to remain the hub of regional diplomacy, not that it controls external powers.

### “The Code of Conduct will automatically solve the South China Sea dispute.”

Incorrect. A Code of Conduct may help manage behavior, but it will not automatically resolve sovereignty disputes, maritime claims, or power politics.

## Key Terms and Simple Explanations

### ASEAN

The Association of Southeast Asian Nations, a regional intergovernmental organization in Southeast Asia.

### Regional Governance

Cooperation among states and actors in a specific region to manage shared problems.

### ASEAN Charter

ASEAN’s foundational legal and institutional document.

### ASEAN Way

ASEAN’s style of diplomacy based on consultation, consensus, informality, non-confrontation, and respect for sovereignty.

### Consultation

Discussion among members before decisions are made.

### Consensus

Decision-making based on broad agreement or absence of strong objection.

### Non-Interference

The principle that states should not intervene in one another’s internal affairs.

### Sovereignty

A state’s authority to govern itself and control its territory and political decisions.

### Peaceful Settlement

Resolving disputes through negotiation, mediation, dialogue, or legal processes instead of force.

### ASEAN Centrality

ASEAN’s effort to remain at the center of regional diplomacy and institution-building.

### ASEAN Community

ASEAN’s framework for regional cooperation across political-security, economic, and socio-cultural areas.

### ASEAN Political-Security Community

The pillar focused on peace, security, political cooperation, and regional stability.

### ASEAN Economic Community

The pillar focused on economic integration, trade, investment, and competitiveness.

### ASEAN Socio-Cultural Community

The pillar focused on people-centered cooperation, welfare, health, culture, environment, and resilience.

### Treaty of Amity and Cooperation

A regional treaty setting out principles of peaceful relations, sovereignty, non-interference, and cooperation.

### ASEAN Regional Forum

A security dialogue forum focused on confidence-building and preventive diplomacy.

### East Asia Summit

A leaders-led forum involving ASEAN and key external partners.

### ADMM-Plus

A defense cooperation forum involving ASEAN defense ministers and dialogue partners.

### Declaration on the Conduct of Parties

The 2002 ASEAN-China declaration on managing conduct in the South China Sea.

### Code of Conduct

A proposed ASEAN-China framework intended to guide behavior in the South China Sea.

### Regionalism

The process of building cooperation, identity, and institutions within a region.

### Economic Integration

Reducing barriers and increasing economic connections among states.

### Transboundary Issue

A problem that crosses borders, such as haze, migration, disease, or maritime pollution.

### Enforcement Gap

The gap between rules or agreements and actual implementation.

### Soft Law

Non-binding or less formal rules, declarations, or guidelines that can still influence behavior.

### Confidence-Building Measure

An action intended to reduce mistrust and prevent conflict.
