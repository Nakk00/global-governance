# Epic 1 Retrospective: Guided Learning Journey

Date: 2026-04-26

Participants: Amelia (Developer), Alice (Product Owner), Charlie (Senior Dev), Dana (QA Engineer), Elena (Junior Dev)

## Epic Review

### Outcome

- Epic 1 is complete: all 7 stories are done.
- The core journey now has the full scaffold, hero entry, navigation shell, narrative layering, recap re-entry, editorial system, and usability hardening that Epic 1 set out to deliver.
- Verification on the implementation record was strong: formatting, linting, type checking, production build, and Playwright smoke coverage all passed.
- This is the first retrospective in the project, so there were no prior retro commitments to audit.

### What Went Well

- The project stayed aligned because the team kept the content model, navigation contract, and section ids centralized instead of letting them drift across components.
- The hero and `journey-start` handoff established a stable entry point early, which made later navigation and recap work easier to reason about.
- Summary-first narrative sections and local disclosure gave the long-form content enough structure to stay readable without flattening the material.
- The recap cues in Story 1.5 made re-entry feel intentional rather than bolted on, especially once the canonical next-step mapping was centralized.
- The shared editorial system in Story 1.6 reduced visual drift across hero, chrome, narrative surfaces, and support surfaces.
- The final usability hardening in Story 1.7 caught the right class of issues: responsive overflow, text-resize behavior, touch targets, and reduced-motion runtime behavior.
- Flow-level Playwright coverage proved valuable because the regressions that mattered were cross-surface, not isolated logic bugs.

### Friction and Lessons

- The navigation, recap, and focus behaviors are one system, not three separate concerns. When one part shifts, the others need to be checked immediately.
- Runtime behavior matters more than stylesheet intent. A motion-safe stylesheet that is not actually imported is indistinguishable from a missing feature.
- Responsive polish needs explicit text-resize coverage, not just viewport checks. The fixed body min-width issue in Story 1.7 was a good reminder.
- The project benefited from shared primitives and tokens more than from one-off patches. When a fix could be centralized, the result was easier to trust.
- Touch and keyboard paths need to be validated on the rendered shell, not inferred from desktop behavior.

### Team Voice

- Amelia (Developer): "Keeping anchors and content data in one source of truth saved us from drift."
- Alice (Product Owner): "The recap cues turned the page into a teachable flow instead of a long scroll."
- Charlie (Senior Dev): "The late responsive hardening showed why accessibility and overflow need to ship with the feature, not after it."
- Dana (QA Engineer): "The Playwright pass caught the exact regressions that would have hurt demo confidence."

### Keep Carrying Forward

- Repo-managed content modules stay the right source of truth for visible page copy.
- Canonical navigation data should continue to drive top nav, progress rail, recap cues, and anchor behavior.
- Shared editorial tokens remain the safest way to keep the experience coherent across future modules.
- Reduced-motion behavior, visible focus states, and no-horizontal-scroll checks should remain first-class acceptance criteria.
- Playwright smoke coverage should continue to verify the experience as rendered, not just as code structure.
