### What Went Well

- The work shipped in thin layers, which kept reviewable scope small and let the team tighten each trust state before moving on.
- Explicit async states reduced ambiguity. Once the response model named the outcomes clearly, the implementation stopped drifting.
- The browser stayed presentation-only while the privileged retrieval and protection logic remained server-side.
- Accessibility and responsive checks stayed part of the acceptance criteria instead of being left for cleanup.
- Review feedback improved the stories by turning fuzzy panel language into stable interaction contracts.

### Challenges and Growth Areas

- The trust states needed sharper boundaries than the early story drafts suggested, especially for weak support versus refusal versus cooldown.
- Focus return and scroll preservation were easy to get subtly wrong across desktop and mobile.
- The protection layer pushed the team to think about anonymous-session identity and state ownership as product concerns, not just backend implementation details.
- The work had to resist over-abstracting shared references or disclosure components before there was a real second consumer.

### Key Insights

- Typed success states reduce UX ambiguity and make review simpler.
- A calm refusal is better than speculative overreach when the assistant is outside scope.
- Inline source support keeps the chapter coherent and avoids turning the closeout into a detached bibliography page.
- Server-side protection only pays off if the browser can render it as a first-class, readable state.
- Clear contracts make validation repeatable and help the team move faster with less debate.
